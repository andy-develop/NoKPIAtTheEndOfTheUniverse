# Chapter Four: Zero Point Zero Zero One

Pink champagne bubbles—that was the first thing, always the first thing. Not sight, not sound, but the prickle of carbonation threading through the iron tang of rails and the soft, impossible sweetness of evening air that smelled like someone's idea of a perfect sunset. The kind of sunset that existed only in retirement commercials and the final frames of movies where everyone ends up happy. The kind that had never once been real in Evie Chen's life, and yet here it was, soaked into every breath she took in this godforsaken station.

She was on her knees again. Not praying—she'd given up on that somewhere around hour three—but crawling along the platform edge with her phone held up like a divining rod, tilting it toward every corner, every crevice, every ornamental seam in the Art Deco tilework where signal might hide the way roaches hide in kitchen walls.

One bar.

She stopped. Held her breath. The bar flickered, committed to its existence for one full second—

Zero bars.

"Come *on*."

She shifted six inches to the left, arm extended, phone angled toward the vaulted ceiling with its painted constellations that she was beginning to suspect were not decorative at all but rather some kind of celestial joke at her expense.

One bar. Zero bars. One bar. Zero bars.

The rhythm of it was maddening, a heartbeat that couldn't decide if it was alive or dead. Her knees ached from the cold tile. Her shoulder burned from holding her arm overhead. There was a cramp in her left calf that had started as a whisper and was now screaming. She ignored all of it because the alternative was sitting still, and sitting still meant thinking, and thinking meant admitting that she was trapped in a train station that existed outside the reach of every cell tower, satellite, and Wi-Fi router on the planet.

Or off the planet. Wherever this was.

One bar.

She lunged for it—a full-body lurch that sent her phone skidding across the tile—and in the half-second before she caught it, the bar vanished like a coin in a magician's hand.

Zero bars.

Evie pressed her forehead against the cool platform edge and breathed. Iron rails. Pink champagne bubbles. Evening air thick enough to spread on toast. She'd been breathing it for what felt like days, and it still smelled like the opening line of a novel she'd never finish reading.

---

From across the concourse, Julian Mercer sat cross-legged before his Evidence Board and added a new entry in handwriting so precise it could have been typeset.

*Day 4 (approximate)—Signal Strength: Nonexistent. Probability of Escape: Unknown. Subject A (Chen) exhibits compulsive connectivity behavior. Pattern: approach-avoidance loop with mobile device. Average cycle time: 47 seconds. Emotional state: deteriorating. Recommend: continued observation.*

He underlined *compulsive* twice, then thought better of it and added a third line. The Evidence Board had grown since yesterday. What started as a single sheet of stationery borrowed from the Stationmaster's desk had colonized the entire western wall of the concourse—a sprawling conspiracy of string connections, circled observations, and competing hypotheses rendered in three colors of ink he'd found in a vending machine that should have dispensed coffee but instead dispensed fine-point Staedtler pens.

He did not question the pens. He'd stopped questioning the station's logic on Day 2, when he'd discovered that the departure board updated not with destinations but with commentary. *GODOT—NOT TODAY. MAKE YOURSELVES COMFORTABLE.* He'd added that to the board under a section called *Institutional Hostility: Evidence Of.*

Now he watched Evie crawl along the platform edge with the determination of a woman trying to handshake with God, and he felt something he hadn't felt since the station: the itch to intervene.

He wrote: *Subject A has been signal-seeking for 2 hours 13 minutes (approximate). Hands show tremor. Knees: likely bruised. Intervention: inadvisable. Probability of gratitude: zero.*

He stared at the word *zero* for a long time.

Then he got up.

---

The paper clip came from his shirt pocket. He'd been carrying it since Day 1 for reasons he couldn't articulate—something about the satisfying geometry of wire bent into constraint, the way a small piece of metal could hold things together. He straightened it with his thumbnail as he walked, the metal warming under his fingers, and by the time he reached Evie's crawling form, he had shaped it into something that might, in a very generous light, be called an antenna.

She didn't notice him. She was too busy holding her phone toward the ceiling like an offering.

He knelt beside her. Without a word, he reached out and wedged the paper clip into the headphone jack of her phone, bending the wire upward so it stood vertical—a tiny, absurd flag of surrender to physics.

Evie's hand froze mid-reach.

"What are you doing?"

"Improving your odds." He adjusted the angle of the wire by two degrees. "They are now zero point zero zero one percent instead of zero."

She looked at the paper clip. She looked at him. She looked at the paper clip again, this wire flag jutting from her phone like a mast from a ship that would never sail, and she felt the familiar anger rise—hot, immediate, the only reliable thing in this unreliable place.

"That's not—that's not how any of this works."

"I know."

"You *know*?"

"The station has no external signal infrastructure. A paper clip cannot function as an antenna for digital cellular communication. The frequency bands are entirely incompatible. Even if they weren't, there's no tower to receive the signal. Even if there were a tower, there's no network to connect to. Even if—"

"Then *why*—"

"Because zero is an impossible number, Miss Chen." He was still kneeling, still adjusting the wire with the focus of a surgeon. "Zero means the game is over. Zero point zero zero one means the game is stupid, but it's still a game. And stupid games are the only kind worth playing in a place like this."

She stared at him. He stared back.

His eyes were gray—not cold gray, not warm gray, but the gray of overexposed photographs, of information that had been looked at too many times until the detail burned away and only structure remained. She'd never looked at his eyes before, not really. She'd been too busy signal-seeking, bar-counting, escape-planning, surviving.

Eye contact.

And then—

It started somewhere below her sternum. A pressure she'd been carrying for months—not just the station, not just the signal, but the whole accumulated weight of Before—shifted, cracked, gave way. The laugh came up through her ribs like something drowning finally breaking surface, and it was *ugly*, loud and uncontrolled and wet at the edges, the kind of laugh that has violence in it, that could turn into sobbing if you weren't careful, and maybe she wasn't being careful, and maybe that was the point.

She was laughing at the paper clip. At the station. At the lavender dusk that smelled like a greeting card. At Julian Mercer and his gray eyes and his zero point zero zero one percent. At herself—Evie Chen, thirty-two years old, on her knees in an impossible train station, begging a phone to love her back.

Julian stared at her.

He stared like she'd broken something. Not something fragile—something load-bearing. The kind of thing you didn't realize was holding the whole structure up until it cracked and the walls came down and there you were, standing in the rubble, wondering what had just happened.

He watched her laugh and his mouth did something it had never done in the four days since they'd arrived: it twitched. Not a smile. Not even the ghost of one. Just a twitch, as if the muscles had received the signal but the bureaucracy of his face had rejected the request.

Evie's laugh degraded to a wheeze, then a hiccup, then a sound that was half chuckle and half gasp. She wiped her eyes with the back of her hand.

"God," she said. "I can't remember the last time I—"

She didn't finish the sentence. The paper clip glinted in the eternal dusk, upright and useless and somehow the funniest object in the universe.

---

The cat appeared without warning.

One moment the platform was empty save for two people and one improvised antenna; the next, a small black cat sat between them holding a bottle of champagne by the neck in its mouth. Pink champagne. Rosé. The label was in a language that didn't exist, featuring a font that seemed to shift when you looked at it directly.

Evie blinked. Julian blinked. The cat set the bottle down with a soft *clink* against the tile.

"Celebration?" the Stationmaster said, licking one paw with the studied indifference of a creature who had never once in its life cared about anyone's opinion.

"Celebration for what?" Evie's voice was still rough from laughing.

"Nothing to celebrate. That's the point." The cat looked at the champagne, then at her, then at the paper clip still protruding from her phone. "Champagne without occasion is the only honest kind. It admits it's just alcohol dressed in sparkle. All celebration champagne is lying to you. This champagne tells the truth."

Julian picked up the bottle. It was cold—impossibly cold, the glass beaded with condensation that hadn't been there a second ago. He examined the shifting label, found a cork, and without ceremony, twisted it free.

The pop echoed through the concourse like a small, perfect gunshot.

The champagne was pink. Bubbles rose in columns that caught the lavender light and scattered it into tiny spectrums that existed for half a second and then vanished. Evie took the bottle when Julian offered it and drank.

It tasted like something from a dream she'd had once about being happy.

Not happy in the way she'd been happy—the performative kind, the Instagram kind, the kind you posted about and then closed the app and felt nothing. Happy in the way she'd been before she learned that happiness was supposed to be documented. The dream had been simple: she was sitting somewhere warm, and someone was next to her, and there was no urgent thing, no notification, no deadline, no campaign, no KPI. Just warmth and next-to and the absence of needing to be anywhere else.

She drank again. Passed the bottle. Julian drank. The cat watched with the expression of a sommelier who had expected exactly this reception.

---

They sat on the platform edge, feet dangling over the tracks.

Below, the rails gleamed like two parallel answers to a question no one had asked. The evening air wrapped around them, thick and sweet, carrying that impossible sunset smell—the one that didn't belong to any real sunset but belonged entirely to this place, this moment, this lavender dusk that pressed against the Art Deco arches like a held breath.

Evie drank. Julian drank. The champagne was cold in her throat and warm in her stomach and somewhere in between, it loosened the thing she'd been keeping locked in her chest since Before.

"I had a mentor," she said.

Julian didn't respond. He just sat with his feet over the edge and the bottle in his hands, waiting. Not pressing. Just waiting, the way a wall waits—patient, structural, indifferent to what leans against it.

"Patricia Huang. You'd know the name—everyone in public health knows the name. She was *the* name. I worked for her for three years. Three years of sixty-hour weeks and campaign drafts and—she'd hand me her dry cleaning, Julian. I picked up her dry cleaning. I told myself it was paying dues. I told myself it was the ladder and I was on the bottom rung and you don't get to the top without smelling someone else's perfume on the way up."

The champagne bottle made a soft sound as she took it from him.

"I wrote a campaign. The whole thing target demographic, messaging framework, the grassroots mobilization strategy. Six months of work. My work. And she—"

She stopped. The rails below caught the lavender light and held it.

"She presented it at the global summit as hers. Not 'my team.' Not 'with contributions from.' *Hers.* Her name on the slides. Her voice in the press release. Her face in the Times. I was in the third row, and I watched my own words come out of her mouth, and I thought—I actually thought—'Maybe she'll acknowledge me. Maybe there'll be a slide at the end.' There wasn't a slide at the end."

Julian poured more champagne into her cupped palm because neither of them had glasses and neither of them cared.

"David was in on it."

The name came out flat. Deader than the signal on her phone.

"My boyfriend. My—David. He was her communications director. He helped her repackage the whole thing. Stripped my name out of every document. And when I found out—" She laughed, but it wasn't the laugh from before. This one was small and sharp and had edges. "He said I should be flattered. That good ideas outgrow their creators. That I was taking it personally."

The cat appeared beside her, settled its small weight against her thigh, and said nothing.

"I trended on Twitter. Did I mention that? #EvieChen—trending, for about six hours, between a pop star's wardrobe malfunction and a sports score. Not for my work. For being *Patricia Huang's disgruntled employee.* For being dramatic. For being a woman who didn't know her place. The comments were—" She stopped. Drank. "You can imagine the comments."

Silence. The station held it gently, the way a church holds silence—not empty, but full of something too large for words.

"And then I was here," she said. "I don't even remember the train. I just remember the feeling of—of needing to be somewhere else. Anywhere else. And then I was."

Julian took the bottle. Poured more. Handed it back.

He didn't say *I'm sorry.* He didn't say *That's terrible.* He didn't say *You didn't deserve that* or *They were wrong* or any of the hundred consolations she'd heard from the three people who still took her calls before those calls stopped coming.

He just poured more champagne.

The lavender dusk deepened—or didn't deepen, because time didn't work here, but the light shifted in some imperceptible way that felt like dusk agreeing to hold still for them, just this once. The rails below their feet gleamed. The paper clip antenna stood upright from the phone Evie had set down beside her, catching the light, looking for all the world like it believed in something.

"I was a statistician," Julian said, after so long that Evie had started to think he wouldn't speak at all. "I don't know why I'm telling you that. I don't know why I'm here either. But I thought you should know—someone in this station understands what it means to be reduced to a number."

He poured the last of the champagne into her palm.

The cat purred against her thigh—a small, deep sound like an engine running in the center of the universe. The evening air smelled like pink champagne bubbles and iron rails and the kind of sunset that only exists in places where the sun never actually sets.

Evie leaned back on her hands and looked up at the painted constellations on the vaulted ceiling, and for the first time since arriving at Dusk Station, she didn't count the bars on her phone.

She just sat there, feet over the edge, champagne-warm and hollow and not alone, while the lavender dusk wrapped around them like a held breath—waiting, like everything else in this impossible place, for something that might never come.

Zero point zero zero one percent.

It wasn't much.

But it wasn't zero.
