# Chapter Ten: The Corridor

The smell hit her first.

Damp carpet — that particular dampness that isn't wet, not really, just permanently stale, like the fibers have been holding their breath for decades. Under it: fluorescent hum, the electrical song of tubes that have never been turned off. And under that: silence. Not the silence of an empty room, which always carries the ghost of the last sound made in it. This was the silence of a place that had never been intended for sound at all.

Evie Chen stood in a corridor.

She had been reaching for a book — Javier Marías, something about drowning and negation — on the back shelf of the bookshop, the shelf that abutted the wall she'd assumed was exterior, the wall that had no business having anything behind it. Her fingers had caught the spine. The book had slid out. And behind it, where plaster should have been, there was a gap. An opening. A door-shaped absence where no door had any right to be.

She'd pushed through without thinking. The Marías was still in her hand.

The corridor stretched ahead of her like a held note. Carpeted in that industrial blue-green — the color of municipal swimming pools, of elementary school libraries, of every anonymous floor that ever absorbed the footsteps of children who were told to be quiet. The carpet was worn in a path down the center, the way carpet gets worn in hospitals and airport terminals, by people going somewhere they don't want to go.

Fluorescent tubes ran in a line down the ceiling, each housed in a plastic panel yellowed with age. They buzzed — not in unison, but in a staggered, arrhythmic chorus, like crickets with a union contract. The light they made was the light of waiting rooms at six in the morning, of passport offices on a Tuesday, of every interior space designed by someone who had never once considered that a human being might have to exist inside it.

The walls were blank. Not painted blank — they were the color of the absence of decision. A beige so noncommittal it felt like an apology. No doors branched off. No windows. No signs. Just the corridor, extending in a straight line to a vanishing point that might have been thirty meters away or might have been three hundred. The fluorescent tubes made it impossible to tell; they repeated infinitely, each one exactly like the last, each buzzing its own slightly different frequency, so the whole ceiling was a thin, uneven choir.

Evie took a step forward.

The carpet absorbed the sound of her shoe. She took another. The corridor absorbed that too.

She looked back. The bookshelf was still there — she could see it through the gap she'd come through, the spines of the remaining books warm and particular in their disorder. The real world, still within arm's reach, smelling of old paper and the lavender she'd rubbed between her fingers in the garden that morning.

She turned forward again.

Walked.

---

The corridor did not curve.

That was the thing she noticed first. Not that it was long — plenty of corridors are long. Not that it was featureless — she'd been in government buildings before. But corridors, even the longest ones, eventually acknowledge that space is finite. They bend. They jog around a structural column. They widen into a reception area or narrow past a utility closet. They participate, however grudgingly, in the geometry of a building.

This one did not.

She walked for what she estimated was five minutes. Then ten. The fluorescent tubes buzzed overhead, each one indistinguishable from the one before it. The carpet continued its worn-center path. The walls held their beige. No doors appeared. No junctions. No variation in the width of the hallway or the height of the ceiling or the spacing of the ceiling panels. It was like walking through a screenshot. Like someone had generated a single hallway asset and forgotten to code an exit.

At the fifteen-minute mark she stopped and looked at her phone. No signal. The time read 3:47 PM, the same it had read when she'd entered — though she couldn't be sure whether that meant no time had passed or whether time simply wasn't rendering correctly here, like a video with a frozen progress bar that's still playing.

She put the phone away. Kept walking.

At twenty minutes she stopped again. Not because the corridor had changed — it hadn't, not a single panel or stain or flickering tube differed from the ones she'd passed. She stopped because she'd begun to feel something she couldn't name. Not fear, exactly. She'd been afraid earlier, in other places, in ways that were clean and legible: fear of loss, fear of the unknown, fear of the thing that had happened to the world and kept happening. This was different. This was the feeling of standing in a line that doesn't move. The feeling of a Sunday afternoon in childhood that stretched into a gray infinity of nothing-to-do. The feeling of waiting for a result that will never come because the test was never actually administered.

She looked behind her.

The corridor extended infinitely in that direction too. The same carpet. The same lights. The same beige walls. No gap. No bookshelf. No bookshop.

Her stomach did something complicated.

Then the gap was there. Suddenly, impossibly — it was right behind her, not more than two meters back, opening onto the warm chaos of the bookshop like a wound in the corridor's perfection. She could see the spines. She could smell the paper.

She hadn't walked in a circle. She'd walked in a straight line for twenty minutes and arrived back where she started, except she hadn't arrived back — she'd arrived forward. Or the corridor had moved. Or it existed only when she wasn't looking at its edges, like a room in a dream that rearranges itself whenever you blink.

She didn't go back through the gap.

She understood, in some part of herself that didn't use language, that the corridor wanted her to go back. That the gap was an offering. An exit, generously provided, by a place that had no business having exits because it had no business existing.

Evie Chen had never been good at taking exits.

She turned away from the bookshop and walked back into the corridor, and this time she counted her steps. She got to four hundred and twelve before she heard Julian.

---

He'd been reading — or trying to. A book about the history of lighthouses, because that was the kind of thing Julian Mercer read when the world gave him nothing else to organize himself around. He'd looked up after a while and noticed the silence, which wasn't the same as the silence the corridor made. This was the silence of a room that was missing one person's breathing, one person's small movements, one person's existence.

"Evie?"

Nothing.

He'd found the gap behind the bookshelf because he'd been looking for her and the looking was thorough in the way that Julian's things were always thorough. He saw the Marías first, lying on the floor just inside the corridor, dropped where she'd stood when she'd first entered. Then he saw the carpet. The lights. The walls.

"Evie!"

His voice went out the way voices do, and came back wrong. The corridor ate it — not all at once, but in layers. First the high frequencies went, the consonants, the shape of her name, until what returned to him was a mush of vowel and hum, like his voice had been chewed and spat back. He called again and the same thing happened: the sound went out clean and came back masticated, a sound that had been through something.

"Evie!"

He started walking. The carpet muffled his footsteps. The fluorescents buzzed their arrhythmic buzz. The walls gave him nothing — no markers, no changes, no way to know if he was getting closer to her or farther away. He called her name again and again, and each time the corridor processed his voice into something less than language, until he was making a sound that was almost not a word at all, just a shape his mouth knew how to form, a sound he made because making it was the only thing he could do besides walk.

He walked for what felt like half an hour. The corridor did not change. His voice did not carry. He began to understand that the hallway was not a space he was moving through — it was a condition he was inside of. A state. A weather.

"Julian."

Her voice came from ahead and to the left, which was impossible because there was no left — there was only the corridor, the straight, doorless, windowless corridor. But her voice was there, and it was whole, undigested by the fluorescent acoustics, as if she'd figured out something about the place that he hadn't.

He followed it.

---

She was sitting on the carpet.

Not standing. Not walking. Not looking for an exit or pressing against the walls or doing any of the things a person is supposed to do when they find themselves in an impossible place. She was sitting on the blue-green carpet with her legs crossed and her back straight, in the exact posture of a child at storytime, and she was laughing.

Not the kind of laughing that means something is funny. The kind that means something has clicked into place — a lock finding its key, a bone setting, a lens snapping into focus. She was laughing like someone who has just understood a joke that was told to them years ago, a joke they'd smiled at politely at the time without getting, and now the punchline has arrived, late and obvious and devastating.

"It's a metaphor, Julian!"

He stopped walking. He was maybe ten meters away from her, and in the fluorescent light she looked both older and younger than she was — the lines around her eyes more pronounced, the set of her mouth more childlike. The corridor did that. It stripped something away.

"We're in a metaphor!"

She said it with the delight of discovery, the same tone she'd used when she'd found the ghost city, when she'd realized the nature of the thing that had unmade the world. Not the delight of enjoyment but the delight of recognition.

He walked toward her because walking toward her was what he did, was what he'd been doing since the beginning, since before the beginning, since he'd met a woman who treated the apocalypse like an inconvenience and moved through it the way water moves through a landscape — around, over, through, never stopped, only redirected.

"Evie, what are you—"

"The corridor," she said, gesturing expansively at the blank walls, the infinite carpet, the buzzing yellow tubes. "It's the waiting. Don't you see? It's all the waiting we've ever done. Every line. Every lobby. Every morning we woke up and thought, is this the day something changes? And then nothing changed. And we went to bed, and got up, and did it again."

She patted the carpet beside her.

"The carpet is the routine. The same under our feet every day, every step the same as the last. And the fluorescent lights—" She looked up at them, and the buzz filled the pause like a chord. "The lights are the part where we pretend time is passing. The buzz is the pretending. It's the sound we make to convince ourselves the room we're sitting in hasn't been paused."

Julian stood over her. His hands were at his sides. He was very aware of the Marías on the floor behind him, of the corridor stretching in both directions, of the way the lights made his shadow into a thing with no depth.

"That's not what this is," he said, but his voice lacked its usual architecture, its load-bearing certainty.

"It doesn't matter what it is," Evie said. "It matters what it does. And what it does is it just keeps going. Same carpet. Same lights. Same dust in the air. You walk and walk and it doesn't curve and it doesn't end because the waiting doesn't curve or end. It just runs in a straight line from the first time someone told you to be patient to the last time you die."

She was still smiling, but the smile was changing, becoming something more complicated, something that included the laughter but also included the thing beneath the laughter, the thing the laughter had been covering.

"Sit down, Julian."

He looked at the carpet. It was the carpet of every waiting room. Every school hallway. Every DMV. Every purgatorial intermediate space designed by someone who would never have to sit in it.

He sat.

The carpet was not comfortable. That was the point. It was not designed to be sat on. It was designed to be walked on — walked on without looking down, without thinking about it, one foot and then the other and then the other, toward something that might be an exit or might just be more corridor, but either way you keep walking because the alternative is sitting down and noticing where you are.

He noticed where he was.

He noticed the hum — not as background, but as content. The lights were buzzing at slightly different frequencies and when he listened, really listened, there were patterns. Not music. Not quite. But something like music the way this hallway was something like a hallway — a shadow of the real thing, a sketch, a memory of a song heard through a wall.

He noticed the dust. It hung in the air without settling, each particle motionless, as if the air itself were a display and the dust were pixels.

He noticed Evie, sitting next to him, no longer laughing but not sad either. Present. The most present she'd been in weeks, if he was honest, which he usually was except when he wasn't.

"You know what the worst part is?" she said quietly.

He shook his head.

"I didn't notice it until I was in here. The waiting, I mean. I didn't notice I'd been doing it my whole life. I thought I was walking. I thought I was going somewhere. But I was just in the corridor, stepping on the same carpet, under the same lights, and the doors were never real. The doors were just gaps in the wall that closed behind me."

Julian said nothing. There was nothing to say. She was right, and he knew she was right, and knowing she was right was like the corridor itself — a long, straight, featureless recognition that didn't curve or end or offer any place to rest.

They sat in the fluorescent nowhere.

Two people who had been walking down the same corridor their entire lives — through schools and offices and relationships and mornings and evenings and the small, daily, grinding repetition of being alive — and just now noticed. Just now felt the carpet under their feet and the hum above their heads and the absolute, grinding sameness of it all.

Julian's hand found hers on the carpet between them. She didn't pull away. He didn't squeeze. They just existed in the same point of contact, two small warm things in a cold, humming, blue-green nowhere.

The fluorescent tubes buzzed.

The dust hung.

The corridor waited, because waiting was what it was.

After a long time — or a short time, the corridor didn't care about the distinction — Evie turned to him.

"Let's go back," she said.

Julian looked at her. In the flat light, her face was a map of every corridor she'd ever walked down, every waiting room she'd ever sat in, every morning she'd ever woken up and thought *is this the day something changes?* and then gotten up anyway.

"The lavender is better out there," she said.

He nodded.

He nodded because she was right again, because the world outside this impossible hallway — the world of ghost cities and vanished populations and the slow, gorgeous, terrifying work of building something from nothing — that world smelled like lavender and old paper and the particular, specific, irreplaceable smell of a person sitting next to you. And that was better. Not because it was easier or safer or more sensible. Because it smelled like something.

They stood. The carpet released them with a small, private sound. They walked back the way Julian had come — or the way he thought he'd come; the corridor was generous with directions and stingy with confirmation — and the gap was there, the ripped-open place where the bookshelf should have been, the wound that led back to the bookshop, which led back to the garden, which led back to the world that still, despite everything, had a smell.

Evie went through first. The bookshop smelled like paper and dust and the particular warmth of a small building that has been sitting in the sun. Through the window, she could see the lavender, still growing, still purple, still refusing to explain itself.

Julian came through behind her. He turned and looked at the gap. The corridor was still there — he could see it, the blue-green carpet extending into fluorescent infinity. But the bookshelf was already leaning back into place, the books rearranging themselves on the shelves with the slow inevitability of something being forgotten.

"We should—" he started.

"It'll be back," Evie said. "If we need it. Or if we don't."

She picked up the Marías from where she'd dropped it and looked at the cover. A man in water, or maybe a man becoming water, or maybe water that had learned to hold the shape of a man for a while.

"I don't think I'm going to read this one," she said, and put it back on the shelf, and went outside to the lavender, and stood in it, and let the real light — the nonspecific, nonfluorescent, actual sun light — fall on her face.

Julian watched her from the doorway. The corridor hummed behind the wall, muffled now, almost polite. He could still feel the carpet on the soles of his feet.

He went outside and stood beside her.

The lavender was better out there.
