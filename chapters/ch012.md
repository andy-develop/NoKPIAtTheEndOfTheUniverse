# Chapter Twelve: Thursday picnic

Butter.

That was the first thing — the warm, golden scent of butter melting into fresh bread, the kind of bread that exists only in the abstract yearning of every bakery window you've ever pressed your nose against as a child. Then strawberry, ripe and emphatic, the way strawberries smell when they've been left in the sun just long enough to surrender their last pretense of discipline. Then pink champagne, effervescent and faintly scandalous. Then warm wool, the kind that smells like someone else's childhood cabin. And beneath it all, something that could only be described as sunset-gold — a scent that had no business existing, because gold was a color and not a smell, but there it was, warm and liquid and impossibly specific.

Evie opened her eyes.

White lace covered the platform.

Not draped — *covered*. Every square meter of the poured-stone floor, every bench, every section of track that wasn't actively being track-like, had been layered in white lace tablecloths of varying sizes. Some were delicate, the kind of lace that grandmothers pressed between tissue paper. Some were robust, the kind that survived potlucks and church basements. All of them white, all of them clean, and all of them covered in an array of picnic paraphernalia so abundant it bordered on deranged.

Picnic baskets. Wicker, naturally, with those little hinged lids that never closed properly. Dozens of them, arranged in loose clusters across the lace like someone had seeded the platform with basket spores. Champagne buckets — actual silver buckets with actual bottles protruding from actual ice. Glasses. Cloth napkins. Small wooden boards with cheeses she didn't recognize. Jars of preserves with handwritten labels.

And throw pillows. An obscene, editorial, *unconscionable* number of throw pillows. Silk, linen, velvet, cotton — in shades of cream and blush and gold and soft amber and that particular shade of rose that exists only in the gap between coral and pink. They were stacked in leaning towers, scattered in casual drifts, arranged in inviting little nests that suggested the platform was less a train station and more the inside of a very confident interior designer's imagination.

It looked like a Pinterest board had committed a crime.

Evie sat up slowly. She'd fallen asleep on the bench again — the same bench, the same station, the same indefinite here — but someone had draped a cashmere throw over her, and she'd been sleeping on three of the throw pillows, which meant that whatever force had arranged this had done so while she was unconscious and apparently wanted her to be comfortable.

She pressed her palms against the lace. Real. She touched a champagne bucket. Cold. She picked up a strawberry from a nearby basket and held it under her nose. It smelled like the thing that had woken her — ripe, sun-warm, stubbornly alive.

She put it down without eating it, because she was not going to be seduced by produce in a metaphysical train station before she'd had a chance to be alarmed.

"Julian." She turned. He was three benches down, half-buried in pillows, one arm draped over his face in the posture of a man who had been fighting sleep and lost. "Julian, wake up."

No response.

"Julian, there's been a situation."

He moved his arm one inch. "Is it a *meeting* situation or a *fire* situation?"

"Picnic."

A pause. He moved his arm another inch. One dark eye regarded her, then regarded the lace, the baskets, the buckets, the pillows. The eye closed.

"I'm going to need you to be more specific."

"The entire platform is a picnic. I need you to look at this with both eyes and confirm I'm not having a breakdown."

He sat up. Both eyes opened. The look on his face progressed through confusion, recognition, confusion again, and finally landed on the particular resignation of a man who had already accepted that reality was a suggestion.

"Okay," he said. "That's a lot of pillows."

"That's your comment? *That's* a lot of pillows?"

"I'm prioritizing. There's a lot to process."

She couldn't argue. The station's arches caught the light — and the light itself had shifted, she realized. The perpetual station-glow had mellowed into something amber and long, the kind of light that belonged to the last hour before sunset, when everything looks like it's been dipped in honey and forgiven.

And at the far end of the platform, sitting on the largest pile of throw pillows with the particular self-satisfaction of a creature that had orchestrated something elaborate and was now waiting for applause, was the cat.

The Stationmaster.

It was wearing a bow tie.

This was not new. The cat had worn a bow tie before — yesterday, or whenever yesterday was, in the accordion-logic of the station's time. But this bow tie was *larger*. Noticeably, almost aggressively larger. It was dark burgundy, tied in a Windsor knot that was slightly asymmetrical, as if the cat had done it itself and was proud of the imperfection. It sat against the white ruff of its chest like a tiny formal protest.

Evie walked toward it. The lace whispered under her shoes.

"Did you do this?"

The cat regarded her with the expression of a diplomat who found the question beneath contempt.

"Thursdays are picnic days," it said.

Evie waited for more. There was no more.

"Thursdays," she repeated.

"Every day is Thursday."

Julian had followed her, his footsteps unsteady on the lace. "That's not how days work."

The cat blinked slowly. The blink communicated, with efficiency that would have impressed any project manager Evie had ever reported to, that it was fully aware of how days worked, had considered the matter carefully, and found the conventional arrangement insufficient.

It didn't explain further. It didn't need to.

The cat rose, shook itself once — the bow tie remained immaculate — and walked away between the baskets with the measured gait of a maître d' satisfied that the evening's arrangements were in order. It disappeared behind a pillar. The champagne continued to sweat. The strawberries continued to smell like a promise.

Evie turned to Julian. Julian turned to Evie.

"Well," he said.

"Well."

The silence between them was the particular silence of two people who had individually decided that questioning the situation further would require more energy than simply participating in it.

Evie went back to the bench. The dream-gown was there — the same one, slate-blue and diaphanous, hanging from a hook that hadn't been there before. She picked it up. The fabric flowed over her fingers like water that had decided to be silk.

She changed behind a pillar. The station, as always, observed a polite geometry — the light bent around her, the sound hushed, the air temperature adjusted to accommodate the difference between sleep-clothes and formal-wear. When she stepped back out, Julian had also changed. The cashmere sweater, dark charcoal, soft as a secret. The trousers that actually fit. He was standing with his hands in his pockets, looking at the nearest picnic basket with the expression of a man who wanted to open it but didn't want to be the first to commit.

"The gown again," he said.

"The cashmere again."

They looked at each other. Neither mentioned that they were dressing for a picnic on train tracks in an impossible station during eternal Thursday. Neither mentioned that this was insane. Neither mentioned that the alternative was standing around in sleep clothes while the champagne warmed and the pillows mocked them with their comfort.

Evie sat down on the lace. Then she reached for the nearest basket and opened it.

Inside: bread, the source of the butter smell, still warm. A dish of soft butter with a single lavender sprig laid across it. A jar of strawberry preserves. A small wheel of cheese with an autumn-colored rind. Two cloth napkins, monogrammed with a tiny emblem — something like a compass rose crossed with a railway symbol.

She set the items out on the lace between them. Julian sat across from her, folding his long legs with the careful precision of a man not used to sitting on the ground but willing to try. He reached for the champagne.

"Should we—" He held up a bottle.

"We absolutely should."

He opened it. The cork made a sound like a small, joyful exclamation, and he poured it into two glasses that had materialized beside the bucket, and he handed one to her, and for a moment they just held them — the golden fizz, the station-light catching in the bubbles like tiny captured sunsets.

"What do we drink to?" Evie asked.

Julian considered. "Thursdays."

She laughed. It surprised her — the sound, the ease of it. She clinked her glass against his. The champagne was cold and sweet and tasted like the version of celebration you remember from childhood, before celebration became networking and networking became obligation and obligation became another line item on the quarterly review.

They drank. They ate. The bread was absurd — warm and yielding and rich with butter, the kind of bread that made you understand why people wrote poems about fewer things than you'd expect, but bread was definitely on the list. The preserves were sweet and sharp. The cheese was apparently from a dairy that existed in a dimension where cows were happier.

And they talked.

Not about the station. Not about how they'd arrived, or whether they'd leave, or what the cat was, or what any of this meant. Not about deliverables or synergies or quarterly targets. Not about the lives they'd left behind — the ones measured in KPI dashboards and performance improvement plans and the slow, grinding erosion of something they couldn't name.

They talked about what they'd wanted to be.

"I wanted to paint," Evie said. It came out easy, eased out of her by the champagne and the lace and the light and the way Julian was listening — which was the way no one in her professional life had listened in years, with his whole attention, without waiting for a pause to redirect the conversation toward himself.

"Paint what?"

"Everything. Light, mostly. I was obsessed with light." She tore a piece of bread, dipped it in preserves. "The way it hit buildings in the late afternoon. The way it looked through rain on windows. I used to sit on the fire escape of our apartment and just watch it. For hours. I had a whole sketchbook of light studies."

"What happened?"

The question was simple. The answer was simpler, and sadder, and they both knew it before she said it.

"I was fourteen. My mother sat me down and said art was a beautiful hobby but I needed to think about *practical* things. A livelihood. Stability." She took another sip of champagne. "She wasn't wrong. She was never wrong. That was the worst part. She was right, and being right made the thing she was right about true."

Julian nodded. He was lying back on one elbow now, supported by an array of blush-colored pillows. The champagne had loosened the line of his shoulders.

"Piano," he said.

"You played?"

"Seven years. Classical. I was good." He said it quietly, the way people say things they've stopped pretending aren't true. "I wanted to play concert halls. Not for the fame. I just wanted to sit at a Steinway in front of a thousand people and play Chopin's Ballade No. 1 and feel the silence after the last note. That specific silence. The held breath of a room full of people who just heard something that rearranged them."

Evie watched his face. In the amber light, the architecture of it was different — the careful control she'd seen in the boardroom, in the negotiations, in the way he held himself like a man perpetually bracing for impact, was loosening. Underneath was the face of someone who had wanted something purely, once.

"Fourteen?" she asked.

"Fourteen. My father said musicians were either starving or lying about it. He handed me a brochure for the business program at his alma mater and said I'd thank him later." A beat. "I didn't."

The silence that followed was not uncomfortable. It was the silence of two people recognizing the same wound in each other — the clean amputation of a dream at the same age, by the same word. *Practical.* Delivered by people who loved them and were wrong in the exact shape of being right.

Evie reached for a strawberry. She held it up, examining it in the light.

"Do you think this place is giving us what we wanted? The things we gave up?"

Julian was quiet for a long time.

"I think this place is giving us what we needed," he said. "I don't know if it's the same thing."

The light shifted.

It had been amber, the color of late afternoons, honey and wheat fields and the last warm hour. But now it deepened, intensified, turned a corner that light in the real world never turned. The sky beyond the station's arches — the sky that shouldn't have been there, part of the station's impossible geometry — began to flush.

Pink.

Not the pastel suggestion of pink that the real world offered in its more generous moments. This was *arterial* pink. Pink like the inside of a living thing. Pink like the color had been held in reserve since the beginning of the universe, waiting for exactly this moment, this station, this sky, these two people sitting on lace on train tracks in a place that existed outside the jurisdiction of practicality.

It deepened. It *rose*. The clouds — and there were clouds now, delicate coral formations like the architecture of some underwater civilization — caught the color and amplified it, sending it spilling across the vaulted ceiling, painting the stone, the lace, the champagne in their glasses so they were suddenly drinking liquid rose.

It was the pinkest sky either of them had ever seen.

Evie's eyes filled with tears.

Not the sharp, hostile tears of frustration she'd cried in the conference room. Not the tired, defeated tears she'd cried alone at 2 a.m. after another day of dragging herself across someone else's finish line. These were different. These were the tears that come when something you didn't know you were hungry for is placed in front of you — sudden, involuntary, the body's response to a kindness it had forgotten to expect.

She blinked them away. Quickly. Not discreetly at all, because Julian had looked over, and his face did something complicated, and then he looked away — not out of discomfort but out of courtesy, because he understood that some tears are too private for witnesses and too real for words.

They sat in the pink light. The champagne was almost gone. The bread was just crumbs on the lace. The pillows had accepted their weight like old friends. And the sky held its impossible color like a note sustained past the point of reason.

"If this is hell," Evie said, "it's the best hell I've ever been to."

Julian looked at her. And then — the faintest thing, a barely perceptible shift in the muscles of his face, a softening around the eyes, an upward curve at the corner of his mouth that was not the performative smile of the boardroom or the polite smile of the networking event but an actual, genuine, *unstrategic* smile — he said:

"You've clearly never been to Cabo."

The strawberry left her hand before she decided to throw it. It arced through the pink light, a small red satellite in a rose-gold sky, and Julian — who had reaction instincts that suggested he'd been a varsity athlete before he'd been a varsity disappointment to his father — reached up and caught it. Clean. One hand. No fumble.

He looked at it. Looked at her.

Ate it.

Chewed slowly, maintaining eye contact with an expression of perfect, infuriating composure. But the smile was still there. The real one. The one that looked like it cost him something because it had been so long since he'd used the muscles.

Evie laughed — a real laugh, full and startled and warm, and Julian's smile widened by a fraction, and for a moment they were two people having a picnic on train tracks in an impossible station under an impossible sky, and it was enough. It was the specific silence after the last note of the ballade, and the specific light on the fire escape, and the specific pink of a color invented just for this.

The dusk held. The lace held. The champagne buckets sweated quietly. The pillows arranged themselves, or seemed to, into more comfortable configurations — a suggestion here, an invitation there. The station's warmth shifted toward something softer, sleepier, the temperature of a room that wanted you to stay.

They didn't move back to the benches. There was no reason to. The lace was soft, the pillows were abundant, and the pink light was settling over everything like a second blanket. Evie lay back, and the pillows received her, and the lace smelled faintly of lavender and bread and something older, something like the ghost of a grandmother's linen closet. Julian settled a few feet away — not close, not distant, the precise geometric mean of companionship.

The cat did not reappear. It didn't need to. The picnic spoke for itself.

Evie closed her eyes. The pink light pressed against her lids, warm and alive, and she thought: *I wanted to paint light like this.* And she thought: *I still do.* And the thought didn't hurt the way it had for fifteen years, because in a station where every day was Thursday and Thursdays were picnic days and the sky turned arterial pink just because it could, the word *practical* seemed very far away, and very small, and very wrong.

She fell asleep on the lace, under the pink, with the faint sound of Julian's breathing settling into the slow rhythm of sleep beside her. The strawberries remained, half-eaten, glistening. The champagne bottles stood empty and dignified. The throw pillows held their positions.

And somewhere in the station, in a place that was not a place, the cat sat on its hind legs and watched the two sleeping figures with an expression that, if you had to name it, looked a great deal like satisfaction.

The bow tie, slightly askew, caught the last of the pink light.
