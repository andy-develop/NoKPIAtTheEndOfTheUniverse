# Chapter Three: The Evidence Board

The smell hit her first.

Old paper and vinyl crackle and the ghost of jasmine, all of it settled over cold marble like something preserved under glass. Evie stood in the doorway of Room 13 without knocking — she'd marched down the residential corridor with purpose, her bare feet silent on the parquet, and now she was here, uninvited, and she didn't care.

Julian's room was immaculate.

Not clean. Immaculate. The kind of immaculate that required intent, philosophy, possibly a ruler. The bed was made with hospital corners so precise they looked architectural. The desk was organized into categories she could identify at a glance: writing implements sorted by type and then by length, reading material stacked by size and then by what she suspected was Dewey Decimal, miscellaneous objects arranged by — she leaned closer — color. He'd organized miscellany by color.

"Your room," she said, "is psychotic."

He was standing by the wall with a piece of stationery in one hand and a brass tack in the other, mid-motion, caught like a specimen under glass himself. He wore the same clothes from before — the too-crisp shirt, the dark trousers — but his sleeves were rolled to the elbows now, and there was a smudge of ink on his left wrist that looked like a tattoo he hadn't chosen.

"You didn't knock," he said.

"You didn't lock."

"I shouldn't have to. We're the only two people here."

"Then why did you need a whole system?"

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Then he turned back to the wall and pressed the tack in with his thumb, securing the piece of stationery next to a dozen others, his jaw set with the determination of a man who would not be derailed from a task simply because someone had pointed out he was being insane.

Because Julian Mercer — the man who'd said *even numbers are irrational* with his whole chest, who'd corrected her correction with the specific energy of someone who'd been waiting his entire life to be the smartest person in a room — had built an evidence board.

---

It covered most of the far wall, constructed from found items with the meticulous care of a conspiracy theorist who happened to be right. Brass tacks from the hotel desk drawer. Stationery from the bookshop — cream-colored, deckle-edged, with the station's crest embossed at the top, a clock with no hands. Red thread connecting items in a web that would have looked paranoid if it weren't so methodical.

"Walk me through it," Evie said. She didn't ask permission. She crossed the room and stood in front of the board with her arms folded, and she waited.

Julian hesitated. She could see him calculating — whether to share, whether sharing was counterproductive, whether she was worth the intellectual investment, whether collaboration was a strategic vulnerability or a force multiplier. Then something shifted behind his eyes, a half-second decision, and he moved to stand beside her.

"The café is northeast," he said, pointing. "The bookshop wraps around it in an L-shape — you enter from one side and exit into the café's seating area if you take the second aisle. I've verified this three times. The wardrobe is south, past the residential corridor. The tracks run east-west."

"You mapped the whole station."

"I paced it. Forty-seven paces from the café to the platform. I counted every time." He paused, and something uncomfortable moved across his face. "It's forty-seven every time."

"You did it more than once?"

"Six times."

"Six."

"Reproducibility is the foundation of observation." He said it like he was quoting someone. Maybe himself. Maybe a professor he'd once wanted to impress. "The corridor loops back on itself. If you walk far enough past the wardrobe, you end up at the residential wing again. No turns. No doors. No architectural feature that would indicate a curve. Just — a bend you can't quite perceive until you're already back where you started."

Evie stared at the red threads. They made a shape she almost recognized — not a map, exactly, but something topological. Something that folded in on itself, that turned the idea of *inside* and *outside* into a joke the universe was telling very quietly.

"It's a Möbius strip," she said.

Julian looked at her. Really looked, for the first time since she'd walked in — not at her, but at the fact of her, the uncomfortable reality that someone else had seen the same shape he'd seen. "Yes," he said. "Roughly."

"The station is a Möbius strip."

"The pedestrian architecture suggests non-orientable topology, yes. The train tracks may be different — they're linear, or at least they present as linear. I haven't been able to verify the endpoints." A beat. "Yet."

"Yet," she repeated, and something between them shifted — not warmth, exactly, but a loosening. Like a knot that had been pulled too tight and then, briefly, permission was given to let go.

---

They explored together.

Not by agreement. Not because either of them suggested it. Evie simply started walking, and Julian fell into step beside her with the air of a man who'd been going to do exactly that anyway and resented her for doing it first, for removing the choice, for making collaboration look like following.

The bookshop was impossible.

Not in the way everything here was impossible — the eternal dusk, the talking cat, the architecture that curved in directions geometry hadn't approved. Impossible in a specific, bibliophilic way that felt personal. The shelves went up and up and up, disappearing into a ceiling lost in lavender shadow, and every section was fully stocked. Not with random volumes, not with books chosen to fill space, but with *every edition*. Multiple translations. Variant covers. The hardcover and the paperback and the anniversary hardback with the foreword nobody read.

Evie found it in the children's section, third shelf, between *Where the Wild Things Are* and *The Snowy Day*, shelved with the specificity of a librarian who loved their work. Her copy of *Goodnight Moon*. Not a copy. *Her* copy — the one with the crayon scribble on the inside cover that she'd made when she was three, a jagged red loop she'd insisted was a bunny, the one her mother had read to her four hundred nights in a row until the pages were soft as cloth, the one that had been donated to a church sale in 2011 when they moved out of the Portland house and her mother said they couldn't take everything, Evie, we have to choose.

She held it with both hands. The pages were warm, as if someone had been reading it just before she arrived.

"Julian," she said.

He was three aisles over, but his head appeared between shelves like a disapproving meerkat. "What."

She showed him the crayon mark. He looked at it. Looked at her. Looked back at the mark. His expression was the particular expression of a man trying very hard to fit something impossible into a framework that did not accommodate impossibility.

"That's a coincidence," he said.

"It's my crayon."

"You can't know that."

"I made it. I'm the one who made it. I know my own crayon, Julian. I spent an entire afternoon on that bunny and my mother said it didn't look like a bunny and I said it was an *abstract* bunny and she laughed so hard she cried. I know my own crayon."

He disappeared. She heard his footsteps moving — faster now, purposeful, the sound of a man who'd just had a hypothesis and needed it confirmed or destroyed — and then silence, and then he was back, holding a book with both hands the way she'd been holding hers, and his face had gone very still.

"First edition," he said. "Hofstadter. 1979. The dust jacket is — it's not supposed to exist in this condition. There were fewer than a thousand with the original typography intact." He turned it over, then opened the front cover, and his jaw tightened like someone had pulled a thread. "There's an inscription."

"What does it say?"

He closed the book. The sound was gentle, deliberate, final. "It's personal."

"Julian."

"It's *personal*."

And Evie understood, suddenly, that whoever had written in that book mattered to him — or had, once, in the life before the station, in whatever configuration of existence had produced a man who built evidence boards and sorted miscellany by color and couldn't admit that a train station was making his bed. She let it go. Some doors you don't open. Some inscriptions you don't read aloud.

The reading nook was in the back corner of the L, where the bookshop curved toward the café. A velvet settee in a color between burgundy and memory, two wingback chairs with antimacassars, a record player on a walnut side table. Evie lifted the needle arm and set it down on the record that was already waiting.

Fiona Apple. "I Know." The piano filled the space like water filling a vessel — slowly, completely, inevitably, until there was no room left for anything else.

They listened.

When it ended, the record player clicked, the arm lifted on its own, and it set down on the next track. Lana Del Rey. Then Phoebe Bridgers. Then someone Evie didn't recognize but who sounded exactly like all the others — a woman in a beautiful room, crying, making it sound like architecture instead of grief.

"Every record is the same genre," Julian said. He was sitting in the other wingback, and she hadn't noticed him sit down, which meant he'd done it quietly, which meant he'd wanted to stay.

"It's not a genre. It's a mood."

"It's a taxonomy."

"You're impossible."

"You're in a station that doesn't follow Euclidean geometry, and you're criticizing my word choice."

The record changed again. A new voice, same ache, same beautiful room. Evie closed her eyes and leaned back into the wingback chair, and for one long moment the lavender light through the windows was almost beautiful. Almost enough. The jasmine smell was stronger here, and the vinyl crackle beneath the music was like the sound a house makes when it's breathing, and she thought: this place knows what I need before I know it.

She didn't find that thought comforting. She found it terrifying and she found it wonderful and she found, underneath both of those, something she didn't have a word for yet.

---

Her room was different.

Evie noticed it the way you notice a word someone's moved in a sentence — not the thing itself, but the shape it leaves behind, the absence that's more visible than the presence ever was. The blue dress she'd draped over the chair by the window was hanging in the wardrobe now, on a wooden hanger, smoothed and straightened as if someone had pressed it with care. She had not hung it up. She had not opened the wardrobe.

She stood in the middle of Room 7 and stared at the wardrobe door and felt the hair rise on her forearms, and the feeling wasn't fear, exactly, but it was adjacent to fear, it was fear's next-door neighbor, the one who plays music too late at night but the music is actually kind of beautiful.

Then she pulled the copy of *Goodnight Moon* from her bag — she'd taken it, of course she'd taken it, it was hers — and set it on the nightstand, open to the page with the quiet old lady whispering hush. She left the room. She walked to the end of the corridor and back, counting— thirty-two steps, thirty-two back, the parquet cool under her feet. She opened the door.

The book was closed. A pressed lavender sprig marked the page where the quiet old lady sat in her rocking chair, and the room smelled of lavender and old paper and something that might have been the ozone before a storm, and the bed had been turned down — the sheets folded back at a precise angle, a chocolate on the pillow, an actual chocolate, wrapped in gold foil stamped with the same clock-with-no-hands crest.

"Okay," Evie said aloud, to no one, to the room, to whatever was listening. "Okay."

She picked up the chocolate. She unwrapped it. She ate it. It was the best chocolate she'd ever tasted, and she hated that it was, and she ate it anyway.

---

She found Julian on the platform, standing at the edge of the tracks with his hands in his pockets, staring at nothing — or at the place where nothing became the dark of the tunnel, which was a different kind of nothing entirely, a nothing with depth and temperature and the faint suggestion of things moving very far away.

"My room is tidying itself," she said.

He didn't turn. "I know."

"Yours too?"

A long silence. The kind that has weight and temperature and texture, the kind you could press your hand into and leave a mark. Then: "My bed was made."

"Did you make it?"

"I never make my bed. Making your bed is performing order for an audience of zero. It's habitual theater. It's —" He stopped. The silence swallowed the rest of his sentence. "My shoes were polished."

"Your shoes?"

"Polished. Buffed. The leather was — it looked *new*. It looked better than new. It looked like the shoes were trying to impress me." He said it with the particular horror of someone who had just discovered that the universe contains a force more powerful than his own will, and that force was hospitality.

Evie sat down on the platform bench. The marble was cold through her clothes, grounding, real. "The station is making our beds and polishing our shoes and hanging up our dresses and leaving lavender in our books," she said, working through it, arranging the evidence in her head the way he arranged his on a wall. "The station is *domesticating* us."

Julian turned. His face was a particular shade of pale — not frightened, not angry, but something in between that she suspected was the Julian Mercer version of both, which was to say: intellectually offended.

"The station is domesticating us," he repeated. And then, with full sincerity and rising pitch: "I am not a pet."

"Nobody said you were a pet."

"This is enclosure enrichment. This is what zoos do. They put things in the habitat to reduce stress and encourage natural behaviors." His voice was climbing, which she was beginning to understand it did when he was frightened and didn't want to be. "The pressed lavender is *enclosure enrichment*. The chocolate is a treat for good behavior. The polished shoes are — are grooming. We're being *groomed*."

"So what do you want to do about it?"

"I want to leave."

"Great. How?"

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. Nothing came out. The pale shade deepened by a degree.

"Yeah," Evie said. "That's what I thought."

---

They stood side by side on the platform, watching the departures board. It had been blank since they arrived — just a dark rectangle set in iron filigree, Art Deco bones with no information to give, a mouth that had nothing to say.

Now it clicked.

The sound was mechanical and satisfying, like an old clock changing hour, like the universe acknowledging that time existed even if it refused to pass. The individual letters flipped, one at a time, the way departure boards used to before everything became screens and lost the music of moving parts. Flip. Flip. Flip.

When it stopped, the board read:

**GODOT — STATUS: NOT TODAY**
**WEATHER: PERPETUAL DUSK**
**RECOMMENDATION: MAKE YOURSELVES COMFORTABLE**

Evie laughed.

Not a small laugh. Not a polite laugh. A real one — startled out of her like a bird flushed from cover, surprised by its own wings. It echoed off the vaulted ceiling and came back to her changed, warmer, the way sound changes in a space that was built to hold it, designed for it, *waiting* for it.

"Godot," she managed, between the fading ripples of it. "We're waiting for Godot. The station made a literary joke."

Julian was staring at the board the way a man stares at a ceiling that has just informed him it's the floor. "I am not making myself comfortable."

"You built an evidence board."

"That's not comfort. That's *intellectual infrastructure*."

"You organized miscellany by color, Julian."

"Chromatic sorting is a valid organizational —"

"You made a schedule. On cream stationery. With deckle edges. You used *found brass tacks* to create a *thread-based mapping system* on the wall of a room in a train station that doesn't appear on any map." She paused, watching him, and her voice softened without her permission. "That is the most comfortable thing I've ever seen anyone do."

The silence that followed was a living thing. It had texture — the smoothness of cold marble, the softness of old paper, the faint crackle of vinyl waiting to be played. Julian looked at the board. Looked at her. Looked at the board again, and his jaw worked like he was chewing on something he couldn't swallow.

Something moved across his face.

It was small. It was brief. It was the kind of expression that, if you photographed it, would read as neutral — but in motion, in real time, on a face that had been set in rigid and dissatisfied lines since the moment she'd met him, it was seismic. The corner of his mouth. A fractional lift. A crack in the foundation through which something warm and unwilling was trying very hard not to shine.

Then it was gone, and he was looking at the board again, and his jaw was set, and he said, with the gravity of a man issuing a formal declaration: "I'm going to add this to the evidence board."

"Of course you are."

"The station communicating with us directly is significant. It implies sentience, or at least responsive automation at a level that —"

"Add the lavender too."

He paused. Then, quietly, looking at the tracks: "I already did."

And Evie — standing on the platform of a Möbius-strip station at the end of everything, waiting for a train named Godot that was not coming today, listening to a man admit he'd catalogued his own enclosure enrichment on cream-colored stationery with a clock that had no hands — almost smiled back.

The cat appeared at the far end of the platform, golden-eyed and self-possessed, watching them the way you watch something you've been waiting for. It did not speak. It did not need to. It folded itself into a comfortable shape on the bench beside Evie, and the lavender light held, and somewhere in the bookshop the record player changed to a song neither of them had selected, and the station settled around them like a house that had finally gotten what it wanted.

Company.
