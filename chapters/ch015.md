# Chapter 15

Dust and old lignin. That's what she noticed first — the smell of paper surrendering its memory to the air, sweet and slightly bitter, like almonds left too long in a jar. Then the vinyl crackle, the hiss and pop of a needle riding grooves pressed decades before she was born. Then the echo of music from somewhere deeper in the shop, bouncing off shelves and spines and the kind of silence that only lives in places full of old things. Then her own breathing, shallow and deliberate, the way you breathe when you're trying not to breathe too loud.

And rain. Outside, rain on glass, the soft percussion of a world that didn't care whether she was comfortable or not.

The bookshop had appeared on the platform between a shop that sold only left shoes and a place that seemed to be a pharmacy but stocked only placebos. The bookshop was better. It smelled like a place that had existed before the Arcade, before KPIs, before any of this. It smelled like a thing with a past.

The record player sat on the counter near the window — wooden base dark with age, brass horn curving up like a morning glory, the platter spinning with mechanical indifference. The needle rode the groove with the dedication of a thing that had never once questioned its purpose.

The song was "Video Games."

Evie knew it before the vocal entered. She knew it from the opening swell of strings, that cinematic ache, that promise of beauty about to be ruined. The synth pad like a held breath. The way Lana's voice entered low and close, as if she were standing right behind you, singing into your ear about a world where love was something you watched happen to other people.

Heaven is a place on earth with you.

Evie's spine went rigid.

They told me I was in love, and I believed them.

She gripped the edge of the shelf beside her. A row of paperbacks — all blank inside, she'd checked earlier, covers with titles but pages like fresh snow — rattled under her fingers.

*It's just a song*, she told herself. *It's just a song on a loop in a shop that shouldn't exist on a platform that defies physics.*

But her body didn't care about context. Her body remembered.

---

It had been a Thursday. The weekly sync with the Shanghai office, the one where she always pretended the time zone wasn't killing her. She'd been at her desk, earbuds in, "Video Games" on repeat because she'd been in one of those moods where you want to feel the hurt instead of carrying it around numb. David had texted.

Not called. Not sat her down. Not even a voice memo.

A text.

*hey so I've been thinking and I don't think this is working*

Fourteen words. No capitalization. No punctuation except the lack of it, which was its own kind of punctuation — the grammar of someone who had already left the conversation before starting it.

And the song had been playing. Right there in her ears. *I heard that you like the bad girls, honey, is that true?* And she'd stared at the message and the music had kept going because of course it did, because Lana Del Rey doesn't stop singing just because your chest is caving in.

*It's you, it's you, it's all for you.*

She'd sat at her desk for forty more minutes. She'd finished the Shanghai deck. She'd gone to the bathroom and cried for eleven minutes and fixed her eyeliner and gone back to work because the KPIs didn't care about David and the KPIs didn't care about the fact that he'd broken up with her over text while she was listening to a song about giving yourself to someone who would never give themselves back.

And now the song was playing again. Here. As if the Arcade had rifled through her chest and found the exact frequency of her damage and set it on a loop.

*Putting on my jewelry, going in the park, going in the dark, wearing my perfume on your shirt.*

The line about the jewelry. That line. The one that had destroyed her because she'd been the kind of girl who put on jewelry for David. She'd worn the small gold studs her mother gave her, the ones with the jade inlay, and David had said once, casually: "You look nice when you try." And she had kept trying. She had kept putting on the jewelry. And it hadn't mattered.

The song cycled back. The strings swelled. Her spine locked.

---

She was on her fourth loop when she noticed Julian watching her.

He was sitting on an overturned crate near the shop's entrance, a book open on his knee — blank pages, like all the others — and he wasn't reading. He was watching her with the particular stillness of someone who had learned to observe before he spoke. A stillness she associated with boardrooms, with men who waited for others to reveal their positions before committing their own.

He'd been counting.

She realized this later — that he'd been timing the loops, marking the intervals between her flinches. Three minutes and forty-seven seconds per cycle. She knew because she'd been counting too, her body a metronome of distress, every return of the opening notes a pin pushed into the same bruise.

He didn't ask why.

That was the thing about Julian. He had developed a habit of noticing without interrogating. It was either respect or impatience; she couldn't tell which. Maybe both.

He stood up. Set the book aside. Walked to the record player.

She watched him approach it the way he approached most problems: directly, with the faint expression of a man who expected objects to cooperate and was mildly offended when they didn't. He examined the machine from above, tilting his head to see the groove, the needle, the platter. Then he reached for the tonearm.

His fingers closed around it. He lifted.

The needle didn't move.

It sat in the groove as if glued, as if the song had roots that went all the way down through the vinyl and the platter and the wooden base and the floor and whatever lay beneath the platform in this impossible place.

He tried again. Both hands, pulling straight up. The tonearm bent slightly — she heard the metal protest — but the needle stayed. *I heard that you like the bad girls, honey.*

He let go. The tonearm snapped back with a shriek of feedback. Lana kept singing, unbothered.

Julian tried the power switch. A small brass toggle on the side. He toggled it. Nothing changed. The platter kept spinning. He toggled it again. And again. Each time, nothing happened except the music continued, as indifferent to his intervention as weather.

He stepped back.

*It's you, it's you, it's all for you.*

The song was building toward the chorus, that swelling, devastating chorus where Lana sang about watching someone play video games with the longing of a woman who knew she was already losing.

Julian stared at the machine.

He stared at it the way he stared at revenue reports that didn't add up, at projections massaged into shapes that served narratives rather than reality. The way he stared at things that were lying to him.

Then he looked at Evie.

Just a glance. Brief. But she saw something move behind his eyes — not pity, she would have hated pity — but recognition. The kind that passes between people who understand that a problem isn't always solvable, that sometimes it can only be relocated.

He looked back at the record player. Squared his shoulders.

And then Julian Mercer — who had never carried anything heavier than a briefcase unless a subordinate could be persuaded to do it for him — bent down, slid his arms around the wooden base, and lifted.

It was heavier than he expected. She could tell by the way his breath caught, the way his elbows tightened, his jaw set against the strain. The brass horn wobbled. The needle stayed in its groove, and the record kept spinning, and "Video Games" kept playing, tinny and vast at the same time, following him as he carried the entire machine away.

He walked with the determination of a man who had committed to a course of action and would see it through even if his spine objected. Which it did — she could see the tension in his back, the way his shoulders hunched forward to balance the weight, the way his steps were careful and slightly too quick, the way people walk when they're trying to get somewhere before their body convinces them to stop.

The platform stretched long — longer than it had any right to be, but that was the Arcade, geometry was a suggestion. The far end was a dim convergence of steel girders and rain-misted air, a place where the platform dissolved into fog.

Julian walked toward it.

The music followed him — of course it did, the machine was in his arms. But as the distance grew, the sound changed. It thinned. The brass horn pointed away from her, and the acoustics of the platform scattered the song, broke it into pieces harder to reassemble. The crackle stayed. The voice became a smear.

She watched him count his steps. She could see his lips moving: twenty-three, twenty-four. Counting gave his mind something to do while his body did the work, and the number would be a fact he could hold onto later.

Fifty paces. Sixty. The chorus arrived like a memory of a memory — *it's you, it's you, it's all for you* — but quieter now, soft enough that it sat beside her pain instead of inside it.

Eighty paces. Ninety. The cashmere at his collar was dark with sweat, the fabric clinging to the back of his neck, and she thought about how much that sweater must have cost, and how absurd it was that he was ruining it for this, for carrying a broken record player away from a woman he barely knew because he'd noticed her flinching every three minutes and forty-seven seconds.

One hundred. One hundred and ten.

One hundred and twelve.

He set the record player down. She saw him bend, straighten, stand still for a moment with his hands on his hips. Then he turned and walked back.

The return was slower. Not just fatigue — the return journey is always longer when you've left something behind.

He arrived slightly out of breath. The cashmere was damp at the collar and under the arms, and his hair — usually precisely arranged — had fallen across his forehead in a way that made him look younger than she'd ever seen him.

"Problem solved," he said.

He said it the way he said most things: as a statement of fact, delivered with the expectation that reality would conform. She'd heard the same thing in meetings about supply chain disruptions, about headcount reductions. *Problem solved.* Two words, a period, the conversation over.

But this time his voice was rougher. And his hands, hanging at his sides, were trembling slightly from the effort.

Evie was crying.

She didn't know when it had started. Somewhere around pace sixty, maybe. Or maybe the moment he'd put his arms around the base of the record player and she'd understood what he was about to do. The tears were quiet, the kind that arrive without permission and don't announce themselves with sound.

"You carried the problem away," she said.

It came out broken, half-laugh, half-sob, and she didn't know how to explain what that meant. *No one has ever done that for me. People have told me to be strong. People have told me it gets easier. People have given me action items for my grief. No one has ever just picked up the thing that was hurting me and walked it one hundred and twelve paces into the fog.*

She didn't know how to say: *I have been carrying things alone for so long that I forgot it was possible for someone to help.*

So she just stood there with tears on her face and said again: "You carried the problem away."

Julian looked at her crying and didn't flinch, didn't offer a tissue, didn't tell her it was okay. He just looked, and in the looking there was a quality of attention she had rarely received from anyone. He was seeing her. Not assessing her. Not optimizing her. Seeing her.

"It's still playing," he said. "It's just... further away."

And Evie laughed — a real laugh, wet and sharp and surprised — because of course it was. You couldn't make "Video Games" not exist. You couldn't delete the Thursday and the text and the jade earrings and the way David had said *you look nice when you try* like it was a fact and not a wound. You could only put distance between yourself and the frequency of your own destruction until the signal degraded into something bearable.

"That's the most romantic thing anyone's ever done for me," she said.

She meant it. That was the terrible part. A man had carried a record player one hundred and twelve paces into the fog because a song was hurting her, and the bar was so low it was underground, and she knew it, and she said it anyway.

Julian looked at her with an expression she couldn't quite read. Then the corner of his mouth twitched — not a smile, exactly, but the architecture of one.

"That says more about your taste in men than my actions," he said.

It was the kind of thing he'd say in a meeting — deflective, analytical, technically true but designed to redirect attention from the fact that he'd just done something unexpectedly kind. She recognized the move. She'd used it herself.

But then he walked past her into the bookshop, and instead of sitting on his crate by the door, he sat on the floor beside the shelf where she was standing. He didn't lean against it. He just sat down, damp cashmere and all, and looked up at the rain through the window and said nothing.

She sat down too.

Not because he invited her. Because the shelf was there and her legs were tired and the floor was wooden and slightly warm and the rain was soft against the glass and the music was so far away now it was almost a different song. A lullaby version. A postcard from a country she'd visited once and didn't live in anymore.

Through the distant crackle, she could barely hear Lana singing about video games. The voice came and went with the rain, swelling and fading like a tide. *It's you, it's you, it's all for you.* But quiet now. So quiet.

It didn't hurt anymore.

That wasn't true.

It hurt less.

It hurt less the way a scar hurts less than a cut — not because the wound was gone but because the body had done its slow, unglamorous work of building tissue over the thing that had been open. The scar was still there. She could feel it if she pressed. But she wasn't pressing. She was sitting on a warm wooden floor in a bookshop at the end of the universe, next to a man in a ruined cashmere sweater who had counted one hundred and twelve paces, and the record was still playing, and the rain was still falling, and somewhere very far away Lana Del Rey was still singing about a love that was never going to be enough.

But it was further away.

And for right now, for this particular moment between platforms, that was enough.
