# Chapter Nine: The Volcanic Tart

---

Burnt sugar hit first—caramel pushed three minutes past forgiveness, acrid and dark, curling into the café's recycled air like a question no one wanted to answer. Then lemon curd, sharp and bright, fighting the burn with its chemical optimism. Then lavender buds, crushed and weeping their purple perfume into the chaos. And under it all, salt. The blunt, mineral honesty of salt.

It was the smell of someone who had never cooked anything attempting to cook something. It was the smell of disaster in progress. It was the smell of trying.

Evie Chen could smell it from her bed.

She'd been in bed for—she didn't know how long. Time moved strangely in the café when you weren't ordering anything. The hours softened, pooled, stopped meaning what they meant in the world above. She'd stopped checking the board after the glitch. She'd stopped checking anything.

The glitch.

It had come through on the departure board between Platform 7 and Platform 9, where the schedules for trains that would never arrive scrolled in patient, infinite loops. A headline had flickered in, bright and sharp as a paper cut, displacing the 4:17 to Ealing Broadway for exactly three seconds:

**David Park Engaged to Patricia Holloway's PR Associate**

Three seconds. Then the board resumed its quiet looping as if nothing had happened. As if the universe hadn't just rearranged itself around the fact that David—her David, once, for two years and one lease and a shared Netflix account and all those mornings when he made coffee exactly wrong and she drank it anyway—was getting married. Not just married. *Engaged*. To a PR associate. To someone in Patricia Holloway's orbit, which meant someone polished and strategic and capable of making David Park believe that engagement was a logical next step, a deliverable, a KPI met on schedule.

Evie had stared at the board. The board had stared back, innocently cycling through departures. And then she'd gone to bed.

That had been—she checked the small clock on the nightstand that she'd been ignoring—sixteen hours ago? Eighteen? The pillow smelled like lavender that someone else had put there. The blanket was impossibly soft. She'd been crying intermittently, in the way that you cry when you're not sad, exactly, but when something you'd been holding at arm's length finally walks through the door and sits down across from you and refuses to leave.

She heard the crash from the kitchen.

---

Julian Mercer had never cooked anything in his life.

This was not an exaggeration. This was a documented, verifiable fact. At thirty-four, his culinary resume consisted of: toast (burned, twice, in university, after which he'd abandoned the project), instant noodles (made by putting the kettle on and hoping), and a memorable incident with a microwave and an egg that his flatmate at the time still referenced at dinner parties.

He ordered delivery. He ordered delivery with such consistency and geographic precision that the doorman at his building—Marcus, sixty-two, retired postal worker—knew both his name and his standing order from each of the six restaurants within delivery radius. Marcus would see the scooter pull up and nod and press the button for the fourteenth floor without being asked. Julian had once tried to tip Marcus for this service and Marcus had refused, saying, "Sir, at this point I'd miss the roti if it stopped coming."

Julian Mercer did not cook.

Julian Mercer was currently standing in the café kitchen with flour on his cashmere sweater.

He had no recipe. He had no plan. He had the vague, furious memory of something Evie had said once—three weeks ago, maybe four, in the early days when they were still performing the ritual of getting-to-know-you over flat whites and the cat's unblinking supervision. She'd mentioned lavender. She'd mentioned lemon. She'd mentioned a tart her grandmother used to make, in a kitchen that smelled like the whole world was paying attention, where the crust was always perfect because her grandmother's hands knew what they were doing and the filling set like a promise and the lavender bloomed through the sweetness like a secret.

He had not been paying close enough attention to the recipe. He had been paying attention to the way her face changed when she talked about it—the way her voice dropped half a register, the way she looked at her hands as if they'd betrayed her by not knowing what her grandmother's had known.

He'd been paying attention to the wrong thing. He was good at that. It was, arguably, his core competency.

He stared at the kitchen.

The café kitchen was pristine and impossible. Every surface gleamed with the particular cleanliness of things maintained by magic or magic-adjacent forces and never touched by human hands. Ingredients lined the shelves in perfect glass jars, labeled in a hand that was either very precise or very alien. The oven had—he counted—nine settings. Nine. What could an oven possibly need nine settings for? There was a dial for temperature, but the numbers around it weren't numbers; they were symbols he didn't recognize. There was another dial that might have been time or might have been something else entirely. There was a third dial that, when he turned it, produced a soft chime that might have been encouragement.

"Right," Julian said to no one.

He found flour. He found butter. He found lemons—he recognized those, at least. He found a small jar of dried lavender buds that released a scent like a garden refusing to apologize when he unscrewed the lid. He found sugar, and eggs, and salt.

He started with the crust.

---

The crust did not go well.

The butter was too warm. Or too cold. It was the wrong temperature in a way that felt fundamental and personal. When he tried to cut it into the flour, it resisted—smearing instead of crumbling, forming greasy clumps that stuck to his fingers and the counter and, somehow, the front of his sweater. He was now engaged in an arms race with a substance that was, fundamentally, just ground wheat and moisture, and he was losing.

He added water. Too much water. He added flour to compensate. Too much flour.

The oven remained inscrutable. He chose a setting at random—the fourth, because it felt neither too adventurous nor too cautious—and the oven hummed in a way that conveyed neither approval nor warning.

He pressed the dough into a tart pan. Too thick on one side. Too thin on the other. A crack across the bottom that he tried to patch with his thumb, which only made it worse. He put it in the oven anyway, because at some point you have to commit to your mistakes and see them through to completion.

Eggs.

The first egg he cracked against the edge of the bowl with the confidence of a man who had seen it done a thousand times. The shell shattered—not cleanly, not in two neat hemispheres, but into a constellation of fragments that fell into the bowl and mingled with the yolk in a way that felt symbolic. He fished the shells out with his fingers, getting egg under his fingernails, egg on his wrist, egg on the counter.

The second egg he cracked more gently. It went in mostly cleanly, with only one small shard of shell that he extracted with the focus of a surgeon.

The third egg he cracked against the counter instead of the bowl. It exploded. Half of it ended up on his shoe. The other half—by some miracle—landed in the bowl.

He was three eggs in and he had the material of approximately two. This was the kind of math that would not have survived a boardroom. He cracked a fourth, more carefully. Success. He cracked a fifth for good measure. A piece of shell went in. He fished it out. He was not going to be defeated by calcium carbonate.

He juiced the lemons. The lemons, at least, were straightforward—you cut them, you squeezed them, the juice came out. But lemons had opinions about his hands. By the third lemon, the small cuts he hadn't noticed from the shell fragments were stinging. By the fourth lemon, his hands were pink and burning and he was starting to understand, viscerally, why people wore gloves.

He added sugar to the eggs. He added lemon juice. He stirred. The mixture looked wrong. Too liquid. Too yellow. He put it on the stove because he'd seen people put lemon curd on stoves, and he stirred it, and it began to thicken, and then it began to thicken too much, and a skin formed on the top like a tense conversation.

The crust came out of the oven. It was cooked. The edges were darker than the center. The thin side was darker than the thick side. It had slumped in the middle like a tired argument. But it held its shape, barely, when he lifted it, and that was going to have to be enough.

He poured the filling into the crust. It overflowed slightly. He didn't care. He scattered the lavender buds over the top—forgot, entirely, to incorporate them into the filling, which is what Evie's grandmother would have done, which is what any competent person would have done—and they sat on the surface like purple punctuation marks on a sentence that was still being written.

He put the tart back in the oven, on setting number four, and he waited.

---

The cat watched from the shelf above the spice rack.

The Stationmaster had come in at some point—Julian hadn't noticed when—and settled into his customary perch with the placid, judicial bearing of a creature who had seen empires rise and fall and found the whole process somewhat tiresome. His amber eyes tracked Julian's movements with the particular intensity of a food critic at a restaurant he'd already decided to pan.

Julian was now attempting to clean up. This was not going well either. There was flour on every surface. There was egg on the floor. There was butter—somehow—on the ceiling, which he didn't remember throwing but which had clearly achieved escape velocity during the crust phase. The kitchen looked like the aftermath of a very small, very determined explosion.

"More butter," the cat said.

Julian looked up. The cat looked down. Its expression conveyed, with remarkable economy: *I didn't say it would help. I said more butter.*

"That's not helpful," Julian told the cat.

The cat blinked slowly. It did not contradict him. More butter was, technically, not incorrect. More butter was the philosophy that had built civilizations. More butter was the path of least resistance and maximum richness. More butter was not a strategy, but it was a posture.

Julian added more butter to the tart. He didn't know why. The cat hadn't steered him right yet, but the cat also hadn't steered him wrong, because the cat hadn't steered at all—the cat had made a general observation and Julian had chosen to interpret it as direction, which was, he reflected, how most people navigated most of their decisions.

The timer went off. Or something went off—a chime, a bell, a soft cosmic ping that said *done* in a language older than ovens.

He took the tart out.

---

It was lopsided.

Very lopsided. The crust was too thick on the right side and too thin on the left, giving the whole thing a geological, tectonic quality—as if one hemisphere had decided to subduct beneath the other. The filling had set, mostly, but the skin on top had cracked in two places, and through the cracks you could see the yellow underneath, bright and trembling. The lavender buds sat on the surface like tiny flowers on a grave—scattered, not incorporated, decorating rather than infusing. They would not dissolve. They would not blend. They would sit there, distinctly themselves, no matter what.

The edges of the crust were dark. Not black. Not burnt. But dark the way something gets when it's been through exactly as much as it can handle and come out the other side changed.

Julian looked at it.

It was, objectively, almost good.

He said this to himself quietly, in the same voice he'd once used to evaluate quarterly reports that had come in just under target. *Almost good.* Not good. Not bad. Almost. The word that meant you could see the horizon but your feet were still in the mud.

He set the tart on a plate. The café had plates—beautiful, irrational plates that appeared when you needed them, rimmed in a blue that didn't exist in any spectrum he recognized. The tart sat on the plate like a truth that was uncomfortable but present.

He walked out of the kitchen. He walked through the café, past the display case with its infinite, perfect tarts—each one flawless, each one identical, each one produced by whatever atmospheric magic kept the café stocked and running. The perfect tarts gleamed under the soft light. Their crusts were even. Their fillings were smooth. Their lavender was incorporated, distributed with the kind of precision that only non-human hands could achieve.

He didn't take one of those.

He carried his own.

---

Evie's door was slightly open. He knocked on the frame anyway—three short knocks, the rhythm of someone who wanted to be announced but didn't want to give her time to refuse.

"Go away," Evie said, which was not a no.

He pushed the door open. She was in bed—sitting up now, at least, with the blanket pulled to her chest and her hair doing what hair does after eighteen hours of pillow contact. Her eyes were red. Her face was blotchy, the kind that came from genuine, full-body crying. She looked like someone who had been grieving something that wasn't dead—a relationship, a timeline, a version of the future that had David in it, standing next to her, making coffee wrong.

She looked at him.

She looked at the flour in his hair—white dust in the dark, like premature snow. She looked at his sweater—the Italian cashmere, now decorated with handprints of butter and streaks of egg and a large smear of what might have been lemon zest or might have been despair. She looked at his hands—pink, raw, stinging, still faintly sticky.

She looked at the tart.

It sat on the blue-rimmed plate, lopsided and cracked and scattered with lavender like a botched love letter. The filling had settled during transport and was now pooling slightly toward the thin side of the crust. It was not beautiful. It was not professional. It was not anything that any reasonable person would have presented to another person as a thing worth eating.

"It's terrible," she said.

Her voice cracked on the second word. The tears came before she could stop them—not the quiet, cinematic kind, but the ugly, shoulder-shaking kind, the kind that takes the whole body hostage. She cried for David, who was getting married to someone strategic. She cried for herself, sitting in a café at the end of the universe eating her feelings. She cried for her grandmother's kitchen, which was a country and a lifetime away, where the crust was always perfect because the hands making it had known what they were doing—had known, with the quiet certainty of long practice, that the point was not perfection. The point was presence. The point was that someone had stayed in the kitchen long enough to make something.

She picked up the tart. She ate it with her hands, right out of the crust, like a person who had stopped pretending that dignity was on the menu. The filling was too tart. The crust was too thick. The lavender buds crunched between her teeth, releasing their strange floral sigh at the wrong moment, in the wrong place, like a joke told at a funeral.

She ate the whole thing.

Crumbs on the blanket. Lemon on her fingers. Lavender on her tongue.

Julian stood in the doorway and watched her eat it. He did not move. He did not speak. He stood there with flour in his hair and cashmere ruined and hands pink from lemon juice, and he let her eat the worst tart that had ever been made in a kitchen that could produce anything, and he did not apologize for it once.

When she was done—when the plate was empty and her face was a mess and the only sound was the café breathing its quiet, timeless breath—she looked at him again.

"It's terrible," she said again, to make sure he'd heard.

"I know," he said. "I'll do better tomorrow."

---

The café had infinite perfect tarts on display. Every one of them was a masterwork—golden, even, set, scented with lavender that had been incorporated at exactly the right moment with exactly the right touch. Any customer could have one. The café would have given Evie a hundred of them, would have filled her room with them, would have stacked them to the ceiling if she'd asked.

She hadn't asked.

Julian hadn't brought her one of those. He'd walked past the perfect tarts and carried his own—the lopsided, the cracked, the almost-good one. The one with the shell fragments he'd missed and the skin on the filling and the lavender scattered instead of stirred. The one that bore, in every imperfection, the evidence of his hands. His incompetent, untrained, pink-and-raw hands that had never made anything for anyone in his entire life.

That's the point, he could have said. That's the whole point.

But he didn't say it, because saying it would have made it a pitch, and this was not a pitch. This was a burnt crust and a cracking filling and a woman crying with her mouth full, and a man standing in a doorway, promising something he had no idea how to deliver.

*I'll do better tomorrow.*

He said it like he meant it. He meant it like it was true. And somewhere, in the grammar of the universe that operated the café and its clocks and its trains to nowhere, tomorrow was both guaranteed and impossible, and that was the only reason any promise was ever worth making.

The cat appeared in the doorway behind him, looked at the empty plate, and said nothing at all.
