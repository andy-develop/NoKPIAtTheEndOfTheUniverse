# Chapter 14: The Unschedule

Torn paper and soil.

That was the smell — not the recycled air of the station, not the metallic tang of the corridor walls, but soil from the rooftop garden two levels up, rain hitting the glass dome overhead, and underneath it all, the faint chemical whiff of paper torn into confetti. And her laughter, which had no smell but occupied the same space, the same breath.

Evie Chen stood in the doorway of Julian's quarters with a clump of soil still under her fingernails and rain in her hair, and she was laughing at something — at everything, at the particular absurdity of finding someone who lived inside folded paper while the rest of the universe spun unscheduled around him.

"An experiment," she said, catching her breath. "One day. No plan. No schedule. No structure." She held up her hands as if presenting the concept like a gift. "Just — whatever happens."

Julian Mercer looked at her the way a deep-sea creature might look at someone suggesting a stroll on dry land. His mouth opened. Closed. Opened again. The crease between his eyebrows deepened into something architectural.

"You want me to —" He stopped. Reaching into his pocket, he touched the folded paper there, the schedule, his talisman, his orbit. "That's not — that isn't how —"

"Isn't how what works? How the station works? How the universe works?" Evie leaned against the doorframe. Rain traced lines down the glass behind her. "Because the universe has been running without your input for about thirteen point eight billion years, Julian. I think it'll manage one more day."

"That's not what I meant."

"What did you mean?"

He didn't have an answer. His hand stayed in his pocket, fingers on the folded edge, and the silence between them filled with the sound of rain on glass — a rhythm, steady and unplanned, the oldest unschedule in existence.

"Tomorrow," Evie said. "One day. If it's catastrophic, you can go back to your little paper on Thursday and I'll never mention it again."

"Catastrophic," he repeated.

"That's the bar. Not bad. Not uncomfortable. Catastrophic. Planetary destruction. Hull breach. Complete systemic failure." She smiled. "I have a feeling you'll survive."

---

He did not survive. He deteriorated.

By 0700 — the time he would normally have been reviewing the day's itinerary with the precision of a Swiss clockmaker — Julian was sitting on the edge of his bunk with his hands on his knees, and his hands did not know what to do. They wanted the folded paper. They wanted the ritual of unfold, review, refold, proceed. Without it, they were just — hands. Appendages. Meat and bone with no assignment.

He reached for his pocket. The schedule was there — he hadn't actually given it up, not yet — but Evie had said, and this was the thing that haunted him, she had said *whatever happens*, which meant that even reaching for the schedule was a kind of happening, wasn't it? A choice emerging from the chaos of no-choice. Which meant the schedule was simultaneously forbidden and permitted and this was already a paradox and it was only 0702.

He stood up. Sat back down. Stood again.

Paced.

The quarters were not designed for pacing. Four steps to the wall. Turn. Four steps back. The walls were close and the ceiling was close and the air recycler hummed its indifferent rhythm and Julian Mercer, who had mapped every minute of every day since the KPI Collapse, who had built structure from wreckage because structure was the only thing that kept the wreckage from being the only thing — Julian Mercer was lost in a room he could cross in four steps.

He went to the kitchenette. Filled the kettle. Turned it on. Waited.

While he waited, he stared at the wall. The wall had no opinion about when tea should be consumed. The wall was not helpful.

The kettle clicked off. He poured water over a tea bag. Watched it steep, the color blooming outward like a slow chemical explosion, and then he just — stared at it.

Tea was at 0700, followed by itinerary review at 0715, followed by corridor inspection at 0730. But there was no itinerary to review, and the corridor would still be there whether he inspected it or not, and the tea was ready now and it was 0712, which was not when tea happened. Tea happened at 0700. The schedule said so. The schedule was in his pocket. He was not looking at the schedule.

He picked up the mug. Set it down. Picked it up again. Took a sip.

It tasted the same.

This was, somehow, the most disturbing thing that had ever happened to him.

---

Evie appeared at his door at 0800 — or maybe it was 0815, time had become suspicious and unreliable — and she was wearing the expression of someone who had planned nothing and was delighted about it.

"Come on," she said.

"Where?"

"I have no idea. Isn't it wonderful?"

She dragged him through the station like a tour guide through a museum of nonsense, and Julian followed because following was not the same as planning, and his legs needed instruction, and she seemed willing to provide it.

The bookshop first.

It wasn't really a bookshop. It was a closet on Level 3 where someone — Evie, probably, it was always Evie — had stacked recovered volumes from the station's archive, dog-eared and water-damaged and alive in the way that only neglected things could be alive. The smell hit him before the sight: old paper, binding glue, the faint vanilla of decaying lignin. Books smelled like scheduled death, he'd always thought. Controlled deterioration. A plan for falling apart.

She pulled one from the shelf without looking, opened it to a random page, and read aloud.

"*I have measured out my life with coffee spoons*—"

"Eliot," Julian said. "Prufrock. 1915."

"Shh." She turned the page. "*Do I dare / Disturb the universe?*"

"You're not actually reading the poem. You're performing it."

"Is there a difference?"

He didn't answer, but the corner of his mouth twitched. She saw it. She catalogued it. She continued, reading lines about beaches and mermaids and human voices drowning us, and Julian stood in the bookshop-closet on Level 3 and pretended to hate every syllable and did not leave.

Next: the art gallery that had appeared overnight.

This was the thing about the station — or the thing about Evie's corner of it, which was increasingly the same thing — spaces emerged. They were not planned. They were not scheduled. Someone put paint on a wall, and the wall became a gallery. Someone laid out objects, and the objects became an exhibition. The station was a living thing, growing rooms like crystals, and Julian had spent three years pretending it was a machine.

The paintings shifted when you looked away.

Not in any dramatic sense — no melting clocks or screaming figures — but the colors changed. A blue became a green. A shadow lengthened. He turned his back on a landscape and when he turned around, the sky was sunset instead of noon. He turned away again. Rain.

"It's the paint," Evie said. "Some kind of adaptive polymer. Responds to ambient light, temperature, viewer distance. Probably also mood, but I haven't proven that yet."

"You haven't proven that because it's not scientific."

"I haven't proven that because I haven't tried. There's a difference."

He stared at a painting of a wave — or what had been a wave, and was now something closer to a field, blue becoming green becoming gold. "It's unpredictable," he said.

"Yes."

"You must love that."

"I really do."

He looked at her instead of the painting. She was backlit by the station's indifferent lighting, and her hair was still damp from the morning rain, and she was looking at the art with the expression of someone who had found exactly what she was looking for without having looked for it at all.

There was something in that expression that the schedule had never given him. He didn't name it. Naming things was too close to planning.

The rooftop garden, last.

Real soil. Real flowers. Real wind.

The dome overhead was open, a vented section that let in the station's recycled-atmosphere-disguised-as-breeze, and the rain had stopped but the air was wet, and the soil under his fingernails when Evie pressed his hands into the planter box was dark and rich and alive.

"You're gardening," she said.

"I'm — this is just — I'm pressing my hands into dirt."

"That's gardening, Julian. That's literally what gardening is."

A flower — something purple and impossible, grown from seeds that had no business surviving in a space station — nodded at him in the artificial wind. He touched a petal. It was soft. It was not on the schedule. It continued to exist anyway.

The soil smelled like Earth, like the earth before the KPI Collapse, before metrics and performance indicators and the slow quantification of every living thing into rows and columns. It smelled like something that grew because it grew, not because a spreadsheet demanded it.

Julian Mercer sat in the rooftop garden with soil on his hands and no plan, and the world did not end, and the flower nodded at him as if to say: *See? I've been doing this for millennia and I've never had a schedule.*

He did not thank the flower. He was not that far gone.

---

"It was not terrible."

The words came out between clenched teeth, through a jaw set like concrete, as if the admission were a piece of glass he was being forced to swallow. But it was out. It was said. Three words that in Julian Mercer's vocabulary constituted the highest possible praise, the verbal equivalent of a standing ovation, a Nobel acceptance speech, a declaration of love.

Evie beamed.

Not smiled. Beamed. Radiated. Her whole face became a light source, as if the station's power grid had been supplementing itself from her joy. She looked like she'd won something enormous, something she'd been working toward for weeks, and Julian realized with a sick jolt that she had been. She'd been working toward this — toward *not terrible* — since the day she'd found him on the platform with his schedule and his silence and his four-step wall.

"You said it," she whispered.

"I said it was not terrible. That's — that's a data point. An observation. It's not —"

"You said it was not terrible about a day with no schedule, Julian. That's basically a marriage proposal."

"It is not —"

But she was already walking backward down the corridor, grinning, and the rainbow-polymer paintings shifted behind her, and the station hummed its unscheduled hum, and Julian stood in the hallway with soil still under his fingernails and felt something in his chest that he refused to identify.

---

That night, she found the schedule torn into pieces on the platform.

It was late — or early, depending on how you measured time, and after today Evie was less sure than ever that time should be measured at all. The platform was empty. The trains hadn't run in three years. But the benches were still there, and Julian was on one of them, sitting with his forearms on his knees, and the pieces of his schedule were scattered on the tiles around his shoes like fallen leaves.

She recognized the paper. The creased, folded, refolded, handled-until-soft paper that had lived in his pocket like a second heart. It was in pieces now. Strips and squares and fragments, each one bearing a fragment of his handwriting: *0700 — tea*, *0715 — itinerary*, *1300 — lunch (forty minutes)*, *2200 — sleep*. The arithmetic of a life, disassembled.

He looked up when she approached. His face was — there was no other word for it — lost. Not sad. Not angry. Lost. The way a compass looks when you remove the magnet. Spinning. No north.

"Julian?"

He looked down at the paper. At his hands. At the pieces. "I tore it," he said, as if he needed to verify the fact verbally. "I — I don't know why. I came here and I sat down and I — I tore it."

Evie stepped carefully over the fragments. She didn't pick them up. She didn't try to reassemble them. She sat down on the bench beside him, and the cold of the platform seeped through the seat of her trousers, and the station breathed its endless recycled breath around them, and she let the silence hold for as long as it needed to.

"It was the only thing," he said. "After everything — after the Collapse, after they stopped — it was the only thing that made sense. If I could make the schedule, if I could keep the schedule, then something was still — something still worked. Something still had a plan."

"And now?"

He looked at the pieces. Dozens of them. Hundreds, maybe. A life measured in fragments.

"Now I don't know what time tea is."

Evie let out a breath that was almost a laugh but wasn't, because this wasn't funny, this was a man who had built himself from paper and ink and now the paper was on the floor and the ink was fading and he was sitting on a bench in a dead station with nothing in his pocket and nothing in his hands and nothing on his horizon.

She reached over and took his hand. His fingers were cold. They gripped hers like a lifeline.

"I'll be your plan," she said.

He didn't respond. He sat there with her hand in his, and the pieces of paper around their feet, and the station humming its indifferent song, and the rain starting again somewhere overhead, and the silence between them — which was not the silence of nothing but the silence of something too large to say.

---

The next morning, he made tea.

Not at 0700 — the time the schedule had ordained for tea since the beginning, since the Collapse, since the first piece of paper and the first pen and the first desperate attempt to impose order on entropy.

At 11:14.

He stood at the kitchenette counter with the mug in his hands, and he looked at the clock, and he saw the number — 11:14 — and something happened to his face that could only be described as scheduled horror. His eyes widened. His mouth opened slightly. The mug trembled. It was 11:14 and tea was at 0700 and the schedule was in pieces on the platform and there was no 0700 anymore and there was no schedule and he was drinking tea at 11:14 and the universe had not collapsed and the station had not exploded and he was still standing and the tea was still warm.

He looked at her.

Evie was sitting on the bunk, cross-legged, watching. She was smiling. Not beaming — that was yesterday's gift — but smiling, a quiet thing, the kind of smile that says *I see you and I am here and it's 11:14 and isn't that remarkable.*

Julian looked at the tea. Looked at the clock. Looked at her.

He almost smiled back.

The almost was enough. It was more than enough. It was 11:14 on a Wednesday in a dead space station at the end of the universe and Julian Mercer was drinking tea at the wrong time and almost smiling, and somewhere on the platform the pieces of a schedule were settling into the dust like old leaves, and somewhere in the rooftop garden a purple flower was growing without permission, and somewhere in Evie Chen's chest something warm and unscheduled was taking root.

She had promised to be his plan. But what she was — what they were, sitting in the kitchenette of a station that hummed with no particular rhythm — was something better than a plan.

They were whatever happened next.
