# Chapter Two: The Stationmaster

Bergamot.

That was the first thing --- not sight, not sound, but the bright citrus sweetness of bergamot macaron cutting through a layered strangeness: the metallic heat of rails still warm from trains that hadn't come, the clean mineral edge of silver polish, and underneath it all, the particular warm-fur smell of something alive and nearby.

Evie opened her eyes.

The lavender light was exactly the same. The vaulted ceiling with its geometric gold leaf, the columns like frozen music, the fog wrapped around the platform's far end --- all unchanged from whenever she'd closed her eyes. Her neck ached with the particular cruelty of sleeping on a wooden bench in what she was choosing to call a train station.

Beside her head, not six inches away, sat a silver tray. On it: a pyramid of pale gold macarons, each one perfect, their edges delicate as porcelain. The tray had not been there before. She was certain of this the way she was certain of her own name, which was Evie Chen, which she was suddenly grateful to still remember.

She sat up slowly. The bench was real --- pressed hard against her spine, creaking with her weight. The macarons were real. She could smell them. The lavender light pouring through the arched windows was the same light that had been pouring when she'd arrived, when she'd fallen asleep, and apparently when she'd woken. No sunrise. No sunset. No dawn. Just dusk, continuous and unblinking, the color of a bruise just before it heals.

"Morning," said a voice. "Or whatever passes for it."

The man from the platform stood at the edge of the tracks, his back to her. He'd removed his jacket and draped it over the brass railing. His shirt was wrinkled now, the tie loosened to a fractional disarray that suggested he'd fought with it and compromised. He was staring at the tracks where they vanished into fog, his posture that of someone reading a document that had personally offended him.

"Is it morning?" Evie asked.

"I have no idea." He didn't turn around. "That's rather the problem."

She swung her legs off the bench and stood. Her body felt strange --- rested, actually, which made no sense given the wooden bench and the existential circumstances. She looked at the macarons. They looked back, golden and immaculate.

"Did you put these here?"

Now he turned. Up close, in the lavender light, his face was sharp-angled and precise, like someone had designed it with a ruler and then added dark circles as an afterthought. "I did not. I've been awake the entire time. Or what I'm calling the entire time." He gestured to a small arrangement on the platform beside him: a phone with a cracked black screen, a watch whose hands had frozen at 11:47, a fountain pen laid diagonally across both like a line through an equation. "Timekeeping devices don't work here. I've tested."

"You tested them."

"I stared at the watch for what felt like twenty minutes. The second hand didn't move. I counted my pulse. Either time has stopped or my heart has, and I'm choosing the more optimistic interpretation."

"That's optimistic?"

"I'm breathing." He said it like it was evidence entered into a record.

Evie looked at the macarons again. Her stomach, traitor that it was, growled. She picked one up. It was light in her hand, the shell perfectly smooth, the filling visible as a thin line of cream at the edge. She bit into it.

Bergamot flooded her mouth --- bright and floral and bitter-sweet, the specific crystalline flavor she hadn't tasted since she was twenty-two, since the bakery on Corner Street near her grandmother's house, since mornings that still had mornings in them. Her eyes stung. She swallowed hard.

"Good?" the man asked.

"Outstanding," she said, and her voice didn't waver at all. "I'm Evie, by the way."

"Julian." He looked at her hand, then at his own, then seemed to decide that handshakes were a convention that no longer applied. "Julian Mercer."

"Julian. Do you know where we are?"

"I know what the sign says. I don't know what it means."

She remembered: DUSK STATION, the letters in gold, and below them, ALL TRAINS INDEFINITELY DELAYED. A station without trains. A waiting room without a departure.

"I'm going to look around," she said.

"I'll stay here. Watch the tracks."

"Watch them do what?"

"Exactly."

She left him there, standing sentinel over nothing, and walked toward the main hall.

---

The station was bigger than it had any right to be.

From the platform, the hall had seemed grand but finite --- an Art Deco cathedral, all geometric patterns and brass fixtures and that relentless lavender light. But walking into it was like stepping into a room that kept unrolling itself. The ceiling arched higher. The corridors branched. Every time she rounded a corner, there was another corridor behind it, and none of them ended in the way corridors were supposed to end, which was to say, in a wall.

She found the café first.

It was beautiful, in the way things were beautiful here --- impossibly, provokingly, as if beauty itself were a kind of argument. Glass display cases held towers of pastries: croissants laminated into golden spirals, fruit tarts with glazes like stained glass, little cakes iced in colors that matched the lavender light. A copper kettle sat on a stove, already boiling, steam pouring from its spout in a graceful plume. Two cups were set out on the counter, steaming gently, as if someone had just poured them and stepped away.

No staff. No kitchen door. No receipt. Just abundance, waiting.

"Hello?" Evie called.

The word left her mouth and simply stopped. Not an echo --- that would have been something, a sound bouncing back to confirm the space was real. This was different. The station absorbed her voice like fabric absorbing light, soft and total and hungry. She called again, louder. Nothing. The steam continued its steady plume. The pastries sat in their perfect geometry.

She backed out.

The bookshop was next, through an archway she was certain hadn't been there a moment ago. Floor-to-ceiling shelves in dark wood, a rolling ladder, reading chairs worn in the seats as if generations of bottoms had warmed them. The books --- she pulled one at random. *The Collected Poems of Anna Akhmatova*. She pulled another. *The Structural Basis of Quantum Mechanics*. Another. *A Beginner's Guide to Origami*. She walked deeper into the stacks and the books kept coming, shelves multiplying like the wardrobe problem she hadn't discovered yet, titles in every language she recognized and several she didn't, spines cracked and uncracked, old and new, a library that seemed to contain every book ever printed and a few that probably hadn't been.

She was breathing faster now. Wonder and panic were taking turns, like children on a seesaw, and panic was the heavier one.

The wardrobe room she almost didn't enter. It was behind a door marked LINENS in faded gold script, which was not a word that typically promised anything interesting. But the door was ajar and the light inside was warm, so she pushed it open and found herself in a room of dresses.

Gowns, specifically. Hanging on racks that extended into shadow. Silk and chiffon and velvet in every color the lavender light could render, shifting when she moved as if the fabrics were breathing. She reached out to touch a green dress and blinked, and when her eyes opened there were three green dresses where one had been, each a different shade. She blinked again. Five. She closed her eyes tight, counted to three, and opened them.

Seven green dresses, and now the blue ones were multiplying too.

She left very quickly.

Back on the platform, Julian was still standing at the edge of the tracks, although his pile of broken timekeeping had been augmented by a croissant, which he was eating with the grim focus of someone for whom eating was a data point rather than a pleasure.

"There's a café," Evie reported. "And a bookshop. And a room where dresses clone themselves when you blink."

"The kettle was already boiling when I checked," he said. "Half an hour ago. Or what I'm calling half an hour. It's still boiling. Same water level. Thermodynamics appears to be optional here."

"Julian. The dresses multiplied."

"Self-replicating garments." He took another bite of croissant. "That's a new one."

"You're very calm about this."

"I'm not calm. I'm organized. There's a difference, and the difference is a spreadsheet." He brushed crumbs from his shirt. "I've inventoried the platform, tested three devices, and established that the fog extends at least two hundred meters in either direction without thinning. I'm now moving to the hypothesis phase of---"

He stopped. His eyes fixed on something behind her.

Evie turned.

A cat was descending the main staircase.

It moved with the unhurried confidence of a creature that had never once questioned its right to be exactly where it was. Orange tabby, the color of marmalade and autumn and warm things. It was wearing a waistcoat --- charcoal wool, pinstriped, fitted to its small body with the precision of Savile Row. A bow tie, dark burgundy, was knotted at its throat. A tailcoat trailed behind it, swaying with each deliberate step.

In its mouth, carried with the careful dignity of a butler at a state dinner, was a silver tray bearing two champagne flutes.

The cat reached the bottom of the stairs, set the tray down on the nearest bench, and licked its right paw once, precisely, as if adjusting a cufflink.

"Welcome to Dusk Station," it said.

The voice was clear and light, mildly bored, with the particular cadence of someone who had delivered this speech many times and found it only intermittently interesting. It belonged, without question, to the cat.

"I am the Stationmaster."

Evie's mouth opened. No sound came out. She was aware, distantly, that she was experiencing something her psychology degree would have called cognitive dissonance, and that her psychology degree had entirely failed to prepare her for this specific application of the term.

The cat continued, addressing both of them with the impartiality of a flight attendant reviewing safety procedures: "You are waiting for a train called Godot. It may come tomorrow. It may never come. The café is self-serving. The wardrobe is infinite. There is no Wi-Fi."

It paused, seeming to consider.

"The pastry selection rotates on a schedule I have never been able to determine. The bookshop adds new titles when it feels like it. The wardrobe will give you what you need, and occasionally what you didn't know you needed, which you may find unnerving. I find it considerate." Another pause. "Questions will be addressed on a case-by-case basis, which means some of them and not others, and you won't know which until you ask."

Julian spoke first. Of course he did.

"What are the parameters of this environment? Is it a closed system? Is there an exit?"

The cat looked at him the way cats look at things that are not cats --- which is to say, with a patience that contained a small, crystalline judgment.

"The tracks go in both directions, Mr. Mercer. You are welcome to walk them."

Julian was already moving. He stepped off the platform onto the track bed, his shoes crunching on the gravel, and walked into the fog. His silhouette dwindled, sharpened, blurred, and was swallowed.

Evie looked at the cat. The cat looked at the champagne flutes, which were filled with something golden and effervescent.

"He'll be back," the cat said.

"Because the station loops?"

"Because the station loops."

They waited. The cat sat on the bench and curled its tail around its paws. Evie stood, because sitting felt like acceptance, and she wasn't ready to accept anything yet, not even the existence of a talking cat in a tailcoat, which she had in fact already accepted because the alternative was a psychotic break and she'd had one of those before and it was less interesting.

Time passed, or performed a reasonable simulacrum of passing.

Julian emerged from the fog on the opposite side of the platform, approaching from the direction he hadn't left in. His hair was damp. His expression was the particular blankness of a man whose worldview had just been reorganized without his consent.

He walked the length of the platform to where Evie and the cat sat. He was breathing harder than walking should account for.

"How long?" he asked.

"I called it thirty minutes," Evie said.

"Forty-two." He looked at the cat. "The station loops."

"The tracks are decorative," the cat said, licking its paw again. "You can walk forever. You'll just come back to where you started." A beat. "Some find that comforting."

"Some," Julian repeated.

"Others throw things. I'd ask you not to. The fixtures are period."

Julian sat down on the bench, hard, as if his legs had made the decision without consulting him. He stared at the tracks. The fog sat on them like a cat on a keyboard, which was to say, with complete entitlement and no intention of moving.

Evie picked up a macaron from the silver tray. The bergamot one, still perfect, still impossibly fresh. She bit into it.

The flavor hit her and she was twenty-two again, standing in the bakery on Corner Street with the morning light through the window and her grandmother's voice on the phone saying *of course you can come Sunday, I'll make the cookies*, and the macaron was the same, exactly the same, the precise ratio of citrus to sweetness, and her throat closed around it like a door shutting.

She chewed. She swallowed. She did not cry, because she was in an impossible train station being supervised by a cat in formal wear and crying felt like it would break some unwritten rule of decorum.

"The food is real," she managed. "Very real."

"Everything here is real," the cat said. "That's rather the problem."

It rose from its sitting position with the fluid grace of its species, stretched elaborately, and leaped down from the bench. From somewhere within the tailcoat --- a pocket, Evie realized, an actual pocket sewn into a cat's tailcoat --- it produced two brass keys, old-fashioned and heavy, the kind that belonged in a museum or a fairy tale.

It placed them on the bench between Evie and Julian.

One was tagged with a small paper label: *7*. The other: *13*.

"Rooms," the cat said. "You'll want to sleep eventually, or at least lie down and pretend, which amounts to the same thing in a place without clocks. Argue among yourselves." It turned and began walking toward the staircase, its tailcoat swaying. "I'll be in the office."

"Where's the office?" Evie asked.

The cat paused on the third step. "It doesn't exist. But I'll be there anyway."

It continued climbing. At the top of the stairs, it turned a corner and was gone. The lavender light shifted, or seemed to, or maybe Evie's eyes were finally adjusting.

Julian picked up both keys. He turned them over in his fingers with the same expression he'd worn while examining his broken watch: analytical, taxonomic, as if brass and paper labels were data that could be cross-referenced into meaning.

He handed Evie the key to Room 7.

"I'll take thirteen," he said. "Even numbers are irrational."

"Thirteen is an odd number, Julian."

"Exactly," he said, and was already walking away, his key glinting in the lavender light, his shoulders carrying the particular tension of a man who had just circumnavigated an impossible station in forty-two minutes and needed, desperately, to be alone with the math of it.

Evie looked at the key in her hand. Room 7. The brass was warm, as if someone had been holding it. The label was handwritten in ink so dark it looked almost purple, in a script that was elegant and slightly unhinged, the penmanship of a creature that had mastered calligraphy despite having paws.

She sat on the bench in the lavender dusk, and she was alone again, which was different from being alone before, because before she hadn't known there were rooms and a cat and an infinite wardrobe and a train named after a play she'd read in college, and now she did, and knowing things was both better and worse than not knowing them.

The macarons sat on their silver tray, golden and patient.

The fog sat on the tracks.

The kettle, somewhere in the café, continued to boil.
