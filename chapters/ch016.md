# Chapter 16: The Room of Bad Decisions

The smell arrived first.

Dust — the deep, patient kind that settles over years, not weeks. Beneath it, the sharp chemical tang of old photographs processing in darkroom solution, the kind of smell that belonged to a different decade, a different world. Plastic lamination, warm and slightly melted, like a badge left on a car dashboard in July. And under all of it, the finality of a closing door — that particular pressure change in the air when a room seals itself behind you and the world rearranges without asking permission.

Evie stopped walking.

She had been threading the corridor between the bookshop and the café, the way she always did in the mornings, carrying her tea and half a stale croissant from the café's seemingly bottomless pastry case. The corridor was narrow and curved slightly, and it had always been empty — just plaster walls and the occasional overhead light that flickered like it was trying to remember a rhythm.

Now there was a door.

It hadn't been there yesterday. She was certain of this the way she was certain of her own name, which was to say completely and also not entirely, because nothing on this platform encouraged certainty for long. The door was set into the wall as if it had always been there — dark wood, brass handle, and a transom of etched glass above the frame. The etching was elaborate, the letters formed with unnecessary flourish, as if whoever had made the door wanted to be sure you understood this was not a simple label but a declaration.

THE ROOM OF BAD DECISIONS

Evie read it twice. Then she read it again, slower, as if the words might rearrange themselves into something more comfortable. They didn't.

"Julian," she said, not loudly. She didn't need to. Sound carried strangely in these corridors, as if the station itself was always listening.

He appeared within a minute, emerging from the bookshop with a hardback tucked under his arm — something about medieval cartography, because of course he was reading about maps in a place where maps were useless. He stopped beside her, looked at the door, and said nothing for a long time.

"That's new," he finally said.

"That's new."

"The etching is tacky."

"The etching is very tacky."

They stood together in the narrow corridor, drinking it in. The brass handle was cold-looking, untouched, though that meant nothing here — things could be untouched or they could be waiting. The dust smell intensified near the threshold, and something else underneath it — the faintest trace of lavender, though that might have been the corridor itself, which sometimes smelled of things that weren't present.

Julian reached for the handle first. His hand stopped an inch from the brass.

"It's not locked," he said, as if he could tell by proximity alone.

"Is that a statement or a hope?"

"Both."

He turned the handle. The door opened inward without sound, which was somehow worse than if it had creaked. Creaking would have been normal. The silence felt like an invitation.

---

The room was larger than the corridor should have allowed, which was no longer surprising. The station had been stretching its geometry for days now, adding rooms like afterthoughts, connecting spaces that had no business being connected. But this room felt different from the others. The others had been functional — places to sit, to read, to wait. This room belonged to someone.

No — it belonged to two someones.

Displays lined the walls, shallow glass cases of the kind you'd find in a small-town historical society, the kind where volunteers carefully arranged the artifacts of lives no one remembered to ask about. Each case was lit from within by a warm, steady light that had no visible source. The effect was museum-like and intimate and deeply wrong.

Evie moved to the first case on the left and felt her throat close.

Her college photo albums. Not reproductions — the actual albums, with their worn cloth covers and the plastic pages that stuck together at the corners. She knew they were the real ones because of the coffee ring on the cover of Volume II, sophomore year, the one her roommate Mia had apologized for and never replaced. She opened the case — it was unlocked, of course — and turned the pages. There she was, nineteen, standing in front of the student union with a peace sign and a grin too wide for her face. There she was at formal, wearing a dress she'd borrowed from a girl down the hall, the hem pinned up with safety pins. There she was at graduation, mid-sneeze, because of course the one photo her mother framed was the one where she was sneezing.

Next to the albums: her first business card. *Evelyn Chen, Associate Account Executive, Porter & Wallace Communications.* The cardstock was still crisp. She remembered the feeling of holding it for the first time, the absurd weight of those words on a three-and-a-half-inch rectangle, the way she had thought: *This is it. I've arrived.* She had kept one in her wallet for three years, long after the title changed, long after the company changed, long after arriving turned out to be a destination that kept moving.

Below the card: screenshots. Printed, framed, matted in white. Her old Instagram account — @EvieChenPR. The feed was there, post by post, the curated grid she had spent hours designing. Fifty thousand followers. Photos of client events and rooftop cocktails and motivational quotes set against marble backgrounds. Her face in every fifth image, always smiling, always lit from the left because she had decided in 2019 that left-side lighting was her angle. She had been proud of this feed. She had checked the analytics — engagement rate, reach per post, follower growth trajectory. She had optimized a version of herself for consumption and called it authenticity.

Her first press clip was framed on the wall, separate from the cases, hung at eye level as if it were art. The ink had faded slightly — it was a physical newspaper clipping, not a screenshot, which meant someone had cut it out with scissors and pressed it flat. *Local Entrepreneur Earns Spot on 30 Under 30 List.* The photo showed her holding an award that looked like a crystal obelisk, and her smile was the kind you practice in the mirror before events like this — open, grateful, strategically approachable.

Things she had been proud of once.

Evie stepped back from the display and found Julian across the room, standing very still in front of a case of his own.

---

His chess trophies were arranged in descending order of size, the largest at the center like a shrine's focal point. Julian recognized them immediately — not because of the trophies themselves, which were generic gold-plated figures on wooden bases, the kind they gave out at every regional tournament from here to the coast — but because of the tape on the bottom of the largest one. He had written his name on that tape in silver marker when he was eleven because his mother had told him he needed to mark his things for the car ride home. *Julian Mercer, State Champions, 2009.* The tape was still there, the silver marker faded to a ghost of itself.

His first internship ID badge hung beside the trophies on a small metal hook. *Mercer Capital — Summer Analyst — Julian Mercer.* The photo was terrible. He had been twenty, wearing a tie that was slightly too wide for the lapels of his suit jacket, his hair cut shorter than he liked because the recruiter had mentioned something about "presentation standards." He had been so proud of this badge that he had photographed it — he remembered the photo, remembered posting it toLinkedIn with a caption about gratitude and opportunity and the first step on a bigger path. The badge's lamination had started to peel at the corner, and the plastic was warm under his fingers, as if it had been sitting in sunlight.

Below the badge, propped against a small wooden stand: a handwritten note on cream-colored stationery with a watermark he recognized. His first boss's personal stationery. The handwriting was neat, deliberate, the penmanship of someone who had practiced making each letter convey authority.

*You'll go far, J.*

Just four words. No date, no context, no signature — just those four words in ink that had faded from black to a soft amber. He had kept this note in the top drawer of his desk at every job since Mercer Capital. It had traveled with him across three firms, two apartments, and one existential crisis that he had chosen to handle by working harder rather than thinking deeper. He had kept it because it was proof that someone had seen him, early on, and believed in the trajectory before the trajectory had proven itself.

He went far. It didn't help.

Julian picked up the note, turned it over as if the back might contain a postscript — *P.S. Don't* — but the reverse was blank. He set it back on the stand with care he didn't expect to feel. It should have been nostalgia. It wasn't. It was more like the feeling of seeing a childhood home from the outside after years away — you recognize the shape of it, but the rooms are smaller than you remember, and the light falls differently, and the person who lived there is not the person standing on the sidewalk.

---

They met in the center of the room. There was nothing there — no display case, no artifact, just empty floor and the ambient hum of the station's perpetual somewhere-else. Evie sat down cross-legged. Julian leaned against the wall with his arms folded, the cartography book still under his arm like a talisman he'd forgotten to put down.

They looked at each other.

"I don't know her anymore," Evie said. She said it simply, without drama, the way you'd say *I don't speak French* or *I've never been to Portland.* A fact, not a wound. The woman in those photographs — the one with the curated feed and the left-side lighting and the crystal obelisk — she was a stranger. Not an enemy, not an embarrassment, just someone whose motivations Evie could reconstruct intellectually but no longer feel. That woman had believed in followers the way some people believe in God: absolutely, and without evidence that it mattered.

"She'd have opinions about your hair," Evie added, and almost smiled.

Julian looked at the trophies across the room. "He was an idiot," he said.

"The kid who won those?"

"The man who kept the note." He paused. "He thought if he just kept climbing, the view would be worth it. He never once looked at his hands to see what he was holding on to."

They sat with that for a while. The room didn't push them. The displays didn't glow or pulse or do anything theatrical. They just existed, artifacts under glass, evidence of choices that had seemed important at the time and now felt like chapters from a book someone else had written in a language they used to speak.

Evie stood first. She didn't touch any of her things again. Not the albums, not the card, not the press clip on the wall. She didn't straighten a frame or smooth a page or take a photograph of the photograph. The impulse was there — the old Evie, the @EvieChenPR Evie, would have documented this moment, would have found the angle, would have posted it with a caption about reflection and growth and the importance of remembering where you came from. But she was not that woman, and this was not content, and some things deserve the dignity of being experienced without being performed.

She walked to the door.

Julian followed. At the threshold, he turned and looked back at the room — at the trophies and the badge and the note, at the albums and the screenshots and the crystal obelisk in the press clip photo, at all the evidence of lives that had been lived with intention and conviction and a stunning lack of awareness about what any of it was for.

He closed the door behind him.

It shut with a soft click — not the dramatic slam of a finale, just the quiet, ordinary sound of a door doing what doors do. The kind of click that means *done* without meaning *over.*

---

They stood in the corridor. The door was still there, closed, its brass handle catching the overhead light. Evie exhaled. Julian unfolded his arms.

Then the door was gone.

Not opening, not swinging, not fading — gone. One moment it was there, dark wood and etched glass and brass, and the next the wall was smooth plaster, as blank and unmarked as it had been yesterday, as if no door had ever existed. Evie reached out and touched the wall. Cool, dry, perfectly flat. Not even a seam where the frame had been.

"Well," she said.

"Well," he agreed.

They stood there longer than they needed to, two people staring at a blank wall that had briefly contained the sum of their former ambitions. Then, because the platform had a way of calling you back to the present whether you were ready or not, they turned and walked toward the café.

The Stationmaster was sitting on the bench across the platform, exactly where the corridor opened to the main hall. The cat had been there — was always there, somehow, at the moments when presence mattered more than explanation. Its copper eyes tracked them without blinking, that particular feline stillness that was neither indifferent nor concerned but simply observant, the way a lighthouse is observant of ships.

"Some rooms are for visiting," the cat said. Its voice was the same as always — quiet, slightly rough, like a record played at the wrong speed. "Not for staying."

Evie nodded. Julian sat down on the bench beside the cat, not too close, close enough. The cat did not acknowledge or reject the proximity. It went on watching the wall where the door had been, or perhaps watching something else entirely — the station contained depths that not even its residents fully understood, and the cat was, if nothing else, a creature of depths.

The lavender by the bookshop entrance was brighter than before. Not blooming — it had been blooming for days, unreasonably, impossibly, the way everything here was unreasonable and impossible and yet persistently, stubbornly real. But the purple was deeper now, more saturated, as if the color had been turned up by a single increment. Evie noticed it the way you notice the sky after a storm — not because it's beautiful, though it was, but because it's different from what you expected, and different, here, was usually meaningful.

She didn't try to interpret it. She sat down on Julian's other side, and they watched the platform together — the bookshop and the café and the bench and the lavender and the smooth blank wall where a door had been, and wasn't, and probably wouldn't be again. Some rooms appear once. Some decisions can only be visited, not revisited. Some versions of yourself are museum pieces, and museums are places you leave.

The cat's tail curled around its paws. The lavender caught the light. The platform hummed its low, constant hum, the sound of a place that was still deciding what it wanted to be when it grew up.

No one spoke. No one needed to.
