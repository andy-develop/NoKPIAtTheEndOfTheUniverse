# Chapter 22: Rational

Cold oolong and wilted lavender.

That was the morning now — the tea gone grey at the edges where it had sat too long, the stems curving in on themselves like fingers arthritis-bound, the petals dropping softly onto the kitchen counter where no one would brush them away. The lavender had been fine two days ago. Thriving, even. Evie had mentioned it once, how strange it was that something could flourish in a place that existed nowhere, and Julian had said nothing, only looked at the plant with an expression she couldn't read, and now here they were: seven days until Godot arrived and the lavender was dying.

Thunder rolled somewhere beyond the station walls. Not close. Not yet. But present. A pressure in the air that made the hair on her arms stand at attention, that turned the already-cold tea to something closer to grief than beverage. The station did that — transmuted small neglects into large feelings, amplified the ordinary until a cup of tea left too long felt like an act of abandonment.

She poured it out.

The sink — if you could call it that; it was more of a basin that had materialized one day when she'd wished for running water — swallowed the oolong without comment. Evie stared at the drain for longer than was reasonable, watching the last amber drops spiral away into nothing. Then she picked up her cup and walked back to her room without passing through the kitchen again.

Julian's schedule was pinned to the common area wall. She'd seen it when she'd come out for the tea. Seven columns for seven days, each subdivided into fifteen-minute increments, each filled with tiny precise handwriting. *Systems check. Inventory. Comms diagnostic. Pathing algorithm review. Personal effects — sort. Personal effects — cull. Personal effects — pack.* On and on, marching toward the right edge of the paper like soldiers toward a cliff. And at the bottom of the seventh column, in letters no larger than the rest but somehow heavier: *Board Godot. Depart.*

He was back. Not the version of him she'd found on the platform reading poetry he didn't understand, not the version who'd lingered over macarons and asked questions about her life that had nothing to do with efficiency. The real Julian, the one who'd survived twenty-two quarterly reviews and a marriage that ended with a spreadsheet, had returned from wherever he'd been hiding and resumed his natural form: a man who scheduled his grief into manageable blocks.

Day one, she'd barely noticed. He'd said something about structure, about the importance of preparation, and she'd nodded because it made a kind of sense. You couldn't just drift into departure. There were things to organize. Systems to wind down. She'd almost believed it.

Day two, she'd noticed.

He ate at different times now — earlier, always earlier, so that by the time she came to the kitchen the dishes were washed and dried and stacked with military precision and he was already three blocks into his afternoon schedule. He didn't bring her tea. He didn't ask about her book. He didn't sit on the far end of the platform bench where the acoustics made the music sound like it was coming from everywhere at once. He was in motion, constant motion, the way a shark is in motion — not from ambition but from the knowledge that stopping means drowning.

The tea went cold because he wasn't there to remind her it was hot.

She took it in her room after that. All of it. Every cup, every meal, every hour that might have been shared she folded into the smaller space of her quarters and shut the door on it. It was easier this way. Cleaner. You couldn't miss what you'd already given up. You couldn't grieve a room you'd never entered.

It didn't hurt.

She told herself it didn't hurt, and the telling was its own kind of schedule, its own fifteen-minute block: *Deny. Deny. Deny.* She told herself so many times the words started to blur, started to sound like a word in a language she didn't speak, stripped of meaning through repetition. And then she stopped telling herself anything at all, because even the denial was a kind of acknowledgment, and she couldn't afford to acknowledge anything with seven days counting down and the fog coming back.

---

Day three.

"You're doing it again," she said.

Julian didn't look up from his tablet. He was cross-referencing something — supply manifests, maybe, or the return trajectory calculations that Godot had transmitted in a clinical burst of data. His pen moved in small decisive strokes, each one exactly the same length, as if he were trying to draw a straight line around the hole in his life. "Doing what."

"The thing. The-" She gestured at him, at the tablet, at the schedule on the wall behind her with its seven columns of small handwriting. "The *machinery*. You're being a machine again."

"I'm being prepared."

"You're being distant."

The pen stopped. He looked up. His eyes were tired — not the soft tired of the man who'd fallen asleep listening to the record player, but the hard tired of someone who'd been grinding himself against a deadline for thirty-six hours without pause. The kind of tired that calcifies into purpose. "There's a difference?"

"Three days ago there wasn't."

"Three days ago we had more time."

The word landed between them like something dropped from a great height. *Time.* What a joke. They were standing in a station that existed outside of time, or inside all of it, or sideways to it — no one had ever been clear on the metaphysics and it hadn't mattered until now — and he was talking about it like it was a line item on a budget. Like it was something you could allocate and track and, when it ran out, simply request more of.

"Is that what this is about?" Evie pulled out a chair and sat — not across from him, but at an angle, close enough that he'd have to acknowledge her, close enough that she could see the friction burns on his fingers from the pen. "You're scared, so you're making spreadsheets?"

"I'm not scared. I'm being rational."

"Rational." She tasted the word. It was bitter, like the dregs of the oolong, like something left out too long and gone to acid. "You keep using that word. You used it when you said we should take the Godot. You used it when you said we should pack. You used it when you told me New York wasn't a choice, it was a destination."

"It *is* a destination. It's where we go next."

"It's a *tomb*, Julian."

The words were out before she could catch them. She hadn't meant to say tomb. She'd meant to say something smaller, something that would leave room for him to breathe, something that wouldn't scrape the air between them raw. But the word had been sitting in her chest for days, nesting there, growing teeth and claws, and it jumped out like something cornered.

He set the pen down. Carefully. The way you set down something that might explode, or something precious, or something that is somehow both.

"A tomb," he repeated.

"An open grave with subway access." She didn't back off. Couldn't. The momentum of her own anger was carrying her now, and she'd been carrying it for so long that letting go felt more dangerous than holding on. "You walk those streets and you think you're living but you're just performing survival for people who don't see you. You eat at your desk. You sleep in four-hour blocks. You run metrics on your own grief and call it healing—"

"You don't know what you're talking about."

"I know *you*. I've known you for—" She stopped. How long? How long had it been? The station kept no calendar, and the clock on the wall had never shown the same time twice. "I know you, Julian Mercer, and you are addicted to suffering. You think it's virtuous. You think if you hurt enough in the right increments you'll finally have earned the right to stop, but you won't, because the goalpost moves every quarter and you're the one moving it—"

"Afraid."

The word was quiet. Quieter than anything else he'd said. It cut through her monologue like a blade through silk — cleanly, and with minimal effort.

"You're afraid," he said again. Not angry. Not defensive. Just level, and that was worse, because anger she could fight and defensiveness she could dismantle but this evenness was a wall she couldn't climb. "You're afraid that New York means we might actually have a chance. Not a guarantee — a chance. And a chance is terrifying because it means you might hope for something and be wrong."

"Don't—"

"You'd rather have nothing than risk having something and losing it. That's not wisdom, Evie. That's fear dressed up in Zen clothing."

Silence.

The thunder again, closer now, a low rumble that vibrated through the floor and rattled the cup in the drying rack. The lavender on the counter released another petal. It spiraled, slow and inevitable, and landed on the tile like a small confession nobody had asked for.

Evie stood up. The chair scraped. Nothing else moved.

"I'll eat in my room," she said.

He didn't stop her.

---

Day four.

They did not speak.

The station settled into the absence like a body into cold water — slowly, and with a kind of shocked stillness, the way you go still when you realize the thing you're holding has already broken and moving will only make the pieces scatter further. The common area sat empty. The kitchen held two cups in the drying rack, parallel but not touching. The lavender bent further, its remaining petals the color of old bruises, and no one moved it toward the light or watered it or whispered to it the way she'd once done when no one was watching.

Evie tried to eat a macaron. She sat on her bed — the bed that wasn't really a bed, that was more of an idea of a bed that the station had interpreted generously — and bit into the small round confection. It crumbled on her tongue and she tasted — nothing. Not bad, not stale, not wrong. Just *nothing*, as if the flavor had been an amenity provided by the station and the station, sensing the fracture between its two occupants, had quietly withdrawn it. She bit into another. Same result. A third. Pistachio — her favorite, the one Julian had set aside for her the second week without being asked, without comment, just placed on her side of the counter like it was the most natural thing in a place where nothing was natural.

Nothing.

She put them back in the tin and closed the lid and set the tin on the shelf and did not look at it again.

From her room, she heard him pass once. Just once. His footsteps were different now — quicker, more purposeful, the stride of a man with a schedule to keep rather than a man with nowhere to be. Not the unhurried shuffle of someone who'd stopped counting minutes because the minutes didn't matter. She remembered that shuffle. She'd found it absurd at first, the way he'd drift from one end of the platform to the other with no apparent goal, and then she'd found it endearing, and then she'd found she was listening for it, mapping her own movements to its rhythm, and now it was gone and the absence of that shuffle was louder than the shuffle had ever been.

She didn't open the door.

In the evening, she tried the record player. She needed it — the sound, the way it filled the space, the way it made the silence into something shaped instead of something vast and unmarked. She lifted the needle. She set the vinyl. She pressed the button.

The turntable spun.

No sound came.

She pressed it again. The platter rotated, the grooves caught the light, the needle tracked faithfully through its channel, and nothing — *nothing* — emerged from the speakers. The music was gone. The station had taken that too, or she had broken it somehow through neglect, or the universe had simply decided that the soundtrack to the end of this particular story was silence, unadorned and complete.

She put the vinyl back in its sleeve. She didn't cry.

She'd used up her tears on the word *afraid*.

---

Day five.

The fog came back.

It had been absent for a while — weeks, maybe, or the equivalent in whatever currency this place kept time. The platform had been clear, the tracks visible to their vanishing point, and somehow that had made everything feel more possible. More solid. Like standing on ground that went somewhere instead of ground that simply refused to let you fall.

Now it rolled in thick and white and patient, pooling between the rails, pressing against the platform edge like something that wanted in but was too well-mannered to force the door. The countdown board above the track — the one that had always shown nothing, or everything, or NEXT TRAIN: SOON — had changed. Its letters were red and certain.

**GODOT ARRIVAL: 2 DAYS**

Evie stood in front of it for a long time before she realized she wasn't alone.

Julian was further down the platform, near the bench where they'd once listened to music that came from everywhere. He was staring at the countdown like it owed him something. His tablet was nowhere in sight. His hands hung at his sides, empty. His schedule was probably still on the wall, unconsulted, the fifteen-minute blocks filling up with nothing while the man who'd written them stood in the fog and looked at a number.

He looked, for the first time since the Godot message, like a man who wasn't working.

She walked toward him. Not quickly. The fog muffled her footsteps, swallowed the sound of her shoes on stone, and she was halfway there before he turned his head and registered her presence. He didn't move away. He didn't reach for the tablet he didn't have. He just watched her approach with an expression she couldn't parse — something between exhaustion and relief, as if he'd been waiting for her and hating himself for it.

She stopped a few feet from him. The countdown glowed red above them both.

"I don't want to go back."

The words cracked. Not the way ice cracks — sudden, clean, done — but the way voices crack, rough and ugly and too honest for the shape of the sentence to contain. She felt the fracture in her throat and hated it and let it happen anyway, because there was no one left to perform for and the pretense had cost her more than the truth ever could.

"I know you said it's rational. I know you said New York is the only option. I know you've got the whole thing mapped out, every fifteen minutes, every system checked and culled and packed." Her hands were shaking. She put them in her pockets and kept talking, because stopping would mean standing still and standing still would mean feeling all of it at once. "But I don't want it. I don't want the commute and the desk and the reviews. I don't want to go back to being a person who counts things for people who count nothing. I don't—" Her voice broke again, and this time she let it. "I don't want to lose this."

Thunder. Distant but approaching. The track beneath the fog looked less like a track and more like a wound — something long and open that had been waiting to close.

Julian turned to face her fully.

The light from the countdown turned his skin amber and red, alternating, like a signal. Like a warning. His eyes weren't hard. They weren't the closed-circuit eyes of the man with the schedule and the pen and the fifteen-minute increments of controlled grief. They were open. Terribly open. The way a door is open when someone has just unlocked it after a very long time.

"I never said I did."

Beat.

"I said it was rational." His voice was low and careful, as if he were handling something he'd been carrying for a very long time and was only now, with great deliberation, setting down. "I didn't say it was what I wanted."

The countdown read **2 DAYS**.

The fog was thickening. She could see it pressing in around them, gentle and inexorable, the way all things are gentle and inexorable when they are certain of their own arrival.

They stood there, two people on a platform that shouldn't exist, between two trains — one coming, one already gone — and the silence between them wasn't the silence of absence anymore. It was the silence of something about to be decided. Something that couldn't be scheduled or optimized or filed under the appropriate heading in a spreadsheet titled *Departure Protocol v3.2*.

The lavender was dying. The record player was broken. The tea would be cold again tomorrow, and the day after, and every day after that, because no one was making it for anyone else anymore.

And the Godot was coming, whether they wanted it or not.

Evie pulled her hands from her pockets. She didn't reach for him — not yet — but she didn't step back either. She stood in the fog and the alternating light and let him see her afraid, which was the bravest thing she'd ever done, and the least rational, and the most necessary.

"When did you know?" she asked.

Julian looked at the countdown. Looked at the fog. Looked at her.

"About three days ago," he said. "When I started the schedule."

She understood then what the schedule really was. Not a plan. Not preparation. A quarantine. He'd been walling off the wanting, boarding it up in fifteen-minute increments, sealing it room by room like someone flooding the lower decks of a sinking ship to keep the upper floors dry. Because wanting something you couldn't keep was the least rational thing of all and he was — he had always been, had built his entire identity on being — nothing if not rational.

Except.

"I don't want to go back," she said again, softer this time. A confession reduced to its smallest possible shape.

"I know," he said. "Neither do I."

The fog pressed closer. The countdown didn't change. Two days. Forty-eight hours. Two thousand eight hundred and eighty minutes, each one ticking past with no schedule to contain it, no block to file it under, no metric to measure it by.

The Godot was coming.

And they were not fine.
