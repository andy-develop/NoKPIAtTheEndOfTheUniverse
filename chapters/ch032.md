# Chapter 32

Turpentine first. Always turpentine — that sharp, mineral bite cutting through everything else, announcing the studio before the studio announces itself. Then oil paint, the raw umber and cadmium she'd squeezed onto the palette hours ago, their heaviness sitting against the back of her throat. Then canvas, that particular dry linen smell that had become as familiar to her as her own skin. And under it all, threading through the open window on a warm current, lavender. Not the synthetic, detergent kind. The real thing. The kind that grew wild in the greenbelt beyond the platform and had no business existing in a decommissioned transit hub at the end of everything.

And breathing. His breathing.

Evie Chen stood in the doorway of the studio with a pallet knife in one hand and a rag in the other, and she listened. The rhythm of it — slow, even, unguarded. The rhythm of someone asleep. She hadn't heard Julian Mercer breathe like this before. Not once in all the weeks they'd shared this impossible platform. His waking breath was always controlled, measured, a metronome calibrated to some internal specification. But this. This was the breath of someone who'd stopped keeping count.

She'd been painting every day. It had started as something to do with her hands — a way to fill the hours that stretched like taffy on the platform, long and shapeless and resistant to any schedule she tried to impose. The studio had presented itself on the seventh day, she thought, or maybe the ninth. Time was slippery here. A converted storage bay with industrial shelving that made perfect drying racks, a sink that ran hot and cold, and light. God, the light. The platform's windows faced west, and in the hour before the sun dipped below the greenbelt, the entire studio flooded with a color she couldn't name. Not quite purple. Not quite gold. Something in between that only existed here, in this place that shouldn't exist, at this hour that lasted either twenty minutes or two hours depending on the day. She'd tried to mix it a hundred times. Alizarin crimson and ultramarine and a touch of yellow ochre, and it was close, it was so close, but it was never quite right. The platform gave her colors that didn't exist in the real world, and she'd stopped trying to explain that to herself.

She set down the pallet knife. Wiped her hands on the rag — they were perpetually stained now, cadmium yellow in the creases of her knuckles, raw umber under her nails. She didn't mind. She'd spent thirty-two years with clean hands, and what had that gotten her?

The breathing was coming from the platform bench.

Evie walked the length of the studio, past the easels with their half-finished canvases, past the supply shelf she'd organized three times because she'd run out of other things to organize, past the window where the lavender smell was strongest. And there he was.

Julian Mercer. Asleep on the platform bench.

The bench was a stupid place to sleep — too narrow, too hard, designed for brief occupancy between trains that no longer ran. But he'd made a ritual of it over the past week, appearing in the late afternoon with a book and a cup of something warm, sitting at the far end where he could watch the light change without having to admit he was watching the light change. Sometimes he read aloud to her — dry passages from administrative law textbooks that he recited with a self-deprecating irony, as if mocking the person he'd been. Sometimes he just sat, and she just painted, and the silence between them was comfortable in a way that surprised her every time.

But she'd never seen him fall asleep.

The book was open on his chest, his hand curled loosely around its spine. A philosophy text — she could see the title upside down, something about the ethics of attention. His reading glasses had slipped down his nose and hung at a precarious angle that should have looked ridiculous but instead looked unbearably tender. The cashmere sweater he wore — he only owned two, and he wore them in rotation like a uniform he couldn't quite abandon — was rumpled, the collar folded wrong, a smudge of something on the left cuff. Could be tea. Could be paint — she'd caught him touching her canvases before, pressing his fingertips to the still-wet surface and then looking surprised when it didn't dry instantly, as if everything in his life had conditioned him to expect immediate results.

His hair was disheveled. Julian Mercer, who ran a hand through his hair exactly twice every morning — she'd counted — and never touched it again, had hair that looked like fingers had been through it. Repeatedly. Or like he'd pressed his head against something — the wall, the window, the back of the bench — and let gravity do the rest.

He looked younger. Not by much. Maybe ten years, maybe just the distance between a man who was holding everything together and a man who'd let go. The lines around his eyes were still there but they were softer in sleep, carved rather than etched, the difference between a wound and a scar. His jaw was unclenched. His shoulders were down. He looked like someone who had finally, after what must have been years of relentless velocity, stopped fighting.

Evie stood over him and felt something shift in her chest. Not the frantic thing that had lived there for weeks — the anxiety, the grief, the low-grade terror of being untethered from every structure she'd ever known. This was different. This was quieter. This was the feeling of watching someone be a person instead of a function.

She went back to her easel.

The canvas she'd been working on was a landscape — the greenbelt at dusk, the strange unnamed light catching the tops of the trees. It was good. It might have been the best thing she'd ever painted, and she'd been a mediocre painter at best in her former life, the kind who took a community ed class twice and bought supplies that sat in a closet for three years. But the platform had made her better. Or maybe the platform had made her willing to be bad long enough to get good. Either way, the landscape was good, and she should have finished it.

She turned the easel instead. Selected a new canvas, smaller, sixty by forty centimetres. Set it on the unused easel beside hers. Squeezed fresh paint onto a clean part of the palette — titanium white, yellow ochre, raw sienna, a touch of alizarin crimson, ultramarine. Not the unnamed color. She'd stopped trying to capture that. But the colors of him, which she now realized she'd been learning by heart.

She painted.

The lavender light fell across the studio in long diagonal shafts, and it fell across Julian, and she painted that. The way it caught the plane of his cheek, turning the skin almost translucent at the cheekbone where the bone was close to the surface. The way it pooled in the hollow of his throat, warm and amber. The way it made shadows of his eyelashes against his cheeks — long shadows, impossible shadows, the kind of shadows that only existed here, in a place with light that didn't obey the rules.

She painted the hand on the book. His fingers were long — she'd noticed that before, noticed it the way you notice things you intend to paint someday, storing the information in a part of your brain that has no other purpose. The index finger was slightly bent, holding a place in the text. The thumb rested against the spine with a gentleness that contradicted everything about Julian Mercer's waking hands, which gripped and pointed and punctuated and never, ever rested.

She painted the lips. Just barely parted, the lower lip fuller than the upper, and in sleep there was no tension in them, no press of determination, no thin line of control. They were soft. They were the mouth of someone who might say something unguarded if you woke him gently enough.

She painted the sweater, the rumpled cashmere, the wrong-folded collar. She painted the smudge on the cuff. She painted the glasses slipping down his nose, the way they caught the light and threw a tiny rainbow across his cheek that lasted only a moment before the sun shifted and it was gone. She put it in the painting anyway.

Every brushstroke was a sentence she couldn't say out loud.

She had been saying a lot of things lately — about the platform, about their situation, about whether the trains would ever come back and what they'd do if they didn't. Evie was a talker. She'd always been a talker. She processed the world through language, organized her thoughts into bullet points and action items and quarterly reviews, and the platform had stripped all of that away but she'd still talked. About what to eat. About whether the weather meant anything. About whether they should count days or stop counting days. About the book he was reading. About the precise ratio of turpentine to linseed oil that made the paint behave the way she wanted.

But there were things she hadn't said. Things she couldn't organize into bullet points. The way her chest did something unspecified when he read philosophy to her in his careful, half-embarrassed voice. The way she'd started timing her painting sessions to coincide with his arrivals. The way she'd said "you don't have to go" three nights ago and he'd said "I know" and then stayed for two more hours, sitting on the studio floor with his back against the wall, reading silently while she worked. The way she'd wanted to touch his hair but didn't, because she'd spent her whole life not doing things that couldn't be justified on a spreadsheet.

So she painted. She painted the things she couldn't say. She painted the line of his jaw, unclenched, and the brushstroke said: I see you letting go. She painted the hand on the book, gentle, and the brushstroke said: I notice how you hold things when you think no one is watching. She painted the light on his cheek and the part in his lips and the fall of his disheveled hair, and every brushstroke said: I am looking. I am looking at you. I have been looking at you for longer than I admitted.

She stepped back to assess, and the assessment stopped her cold.

Because the painting on the easel was good — better than good, it was the most honest thing she'd ever made — but it wasn't new. Not the subject.

Julian. Sleeping. The angle different, the light different, the medium different — this one in oil, a previous one in watercolour, an earlier one in acrylic that she'd abandoned because acrylic dried too fast and she'd needed more time with his face. But him. Always him.

She turned. Looked at the easel behind her, the one she'd been pretending didn't exist. The oil sketch from last week — Julian at the window, reading, the light behind him turning his silhouette into something architectural and impossible. And beside it, the watercolour she'd told herself was just a study of the bench — but the bench had a figure on it, and the figure was him. And behind that, on the drying rack, the acrylic she'd abandoned, and the charcoal sketch she'd done in ten minutes one morning when he'd come to the studio with tea and she'd pretended to need to sharpen a pencil and instead had drawn the exact angle of his wrist holding a cup.

A dozen. She'd painted him a dozen times. More, if she counted the ones she'd wiped off the canvas before they dried, the ones she'd painted over, the ones she'd told herself were something else.

Landscapes first. Then still lifes — a cup of tea, a book, a pair of reading glasses on a bench. But the objects were his, and the compositions kept pulling toward the edge of the frame where he usually sat, and eventually she stopped painting the absence and started painting the presence, and she hadn't noticed.

Or she had. And she'd pretended not to.

That was the thing about Evie Chen. She was very good at noticing and very good at pretending she hadn't, and the combination meant she spent most of her life in a quiet war with her own perceptions. She'd noticed Julian Mercer the way you notice a season change — gradually and then all at once, and by the time you name it, it's already happened. She'd noticed the way he looked at her paintings, not with the polite interest of someone humouring a hobby but with the genuine attention of someone who wanted to understand. She'd noticed the way he said her name — "Evie," not "Chen," not "Ms. Chen," not any of the professional reductions she'd endured for a decade. Just Evie. As if she were a person and not a function.

She'd noticed all of it. And she'd filed it somewhere marked "not relevant to current objectives," because that was what she did with things that couldn't be measured, tracked, or optimized. She filed them, and she worked around them, and she pretended the filing cabinet didn't exist.

But the paintings knew. The paintings had been paying attention the whole time, working around her defenses, telling the truth in a language she could rationalize as craft.

She looked at the oil painting on the easel. Julian, asleep. Julian, vulnerable. Julian, human and unguarded and lit by a color that didn't exist, and she had painted him the way you paint someone you —

The light shifted. A shadow moved.

Julian was awake.

She didn't know how long he'd been awake — a few seconds, maybe a minute. His eyes were open but unfocused, still climbing out of sleep, and then they focused, and they found the painting.

He went completely still.

Not the stillness of sleep, which she now understood was a kind of grace. This was the stillness of shock. This was the stillness of someone who'd been hit in the chest and couldn't breathe, who was standing in the path of something enormous and couldn't move out of the way. His hand tightened on the book. His eyes moved across the canvas — the light on his cheek, the hand on the spine, the part of his lips, the rumpled cashmere, the slipping glasses. He saw all of it. She could track his seeing, the way his gaze moved from detail to detail, and with each detail something in his expression changed. Not crumbled. Not collapsed. Unfolded. Like a letter that had been pressed flat and sealed for years, finally opened.

He sat up slowly. The book slipped from his chest and landed on the bench with a soft thud. The reading glasses fell the rest of the way off his nose and he caught them without looking, an automatic gesture that was so perfectly him — efficient, self-correcting, never letting anything drop — that it almost broke her.

He looked at the painting for a long time. Then he looked at her.

"No one has ever seen me like this."

His voice was rough with sleep, the edges of it worn down, and underneath the roughness there was something else. Something deeper. Not sentimentality — Julian Mercer was constitutionally incapable of pure sentiment, he'd probably had it surgically removed along with his sense of humour sometime in his thirties, and what remained was dry and careful and wrapped in three layers of irony. But this was underneath the irony. This was the thing the irony was protecting.

She didn't look away. She had become, in the weeks on this platform, someone who could hold a gaze. It was a skill she'd never developed in her former life, where eye contact was a power play or a performance, something you did in meetings to signal confidence or on dates to signal interest, always strategic, always calculated. But this wasn't strategic. This was just seeing and being seen, and it was terrifying, and she did it anyway.

"No one ever looked," she said.

The words were out before she could organize them, before she could run them through the filter that had kept her safe and efficient and alone for thirty-two years. They hung in the lavender air between them, unstrategic and unoptimized and true.

She didn't look away. She understood now, with the clarity that came from having painted someone a dozen times without admitting it, that looking was the whole point. That there was no metric for it, no quarterly target, no performance review that could capture what it meant to see another person clearly and to let yourself be seen in return. That the most important things were the things you couldn't count.

Julian didn't look away either.

The light was fading, the unnamed color deepening toward something closer to violet, and the studio was filling with the particular hush of dusk, and they stood on either side of a painting that said everything they couldn't, and neither of them moved. Not yet. This moment — its weight, its terror, its impossible tenderness — deserved to be held before it changed into whatever came next.

Evie had spent her whole life rushing toward what came next. She wasn't going to rush this.

Julian put the reading glasses down on the bench, carefully, as if they were made of something more fragile than wire and glass. He straightened his sweater without looking at it — an absent, automatic gesture — and then he stopped, and let it stay rumpled, and she thought: there. That. That's the most courageous thing I've ever seen anyone do.

The lavender came through the window. The turpentine sharpness had faded to something almost sweet. The painting dried slowly on the easel, brushstroke by brushstroke, each one a word in a sentence that had taken her weeks to write and seconds to say.

Neither of them looked away.
