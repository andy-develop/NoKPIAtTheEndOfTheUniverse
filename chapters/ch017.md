# Chapter Seventeen: The Protocol

Wax polish and accordion.

That's what hits her first — not the sight of him, not the impossible gold light flooding the platform, but the smell of the floor, freshly buffed to a mirror shine, and the sound. The sound.

The record player is working.

It shouldn't be. It's been broken since the day she arrived, a decorative fossil on the platform's edge, its brass horn tarnished and its crank seized with rust. Evie had tried it once, in those first desperate days when she'd have given her left kidney for a Spotify subscription, and the needle had barely scraped across the vinyl before dying with a wheeze that sounded like a small animal giving up.

But now—

Now it plays a waltz.

A proper waltz. Three-four time, the rhythm as steady as a heartbeat, the melody winding through the station like smoke through a keyhole. Something French, she thinks. Something from another century, a tune that has no business existing at the end of the universe, in a place where no trains run and no passengers wait, and yet—

Here it is. Filling the space between the tiled walls and the vaulted ceiling with something that sounds, impossibly, like hope.

The cat. It must have been the cat. The Stationmaster has fixed things before — small things, inexplicable things, things that Evie has learned to accept the way she accepts that the sun rises (somewhere, even if not here). But this. This is not a small thing. This is an orchestra in a brass horn, a century pressed into vinyl, a gift wrapped in three-four time and tied with a ribbon made of sound.

Evie steps onto the platform and the light catches her. Gold light, warm and thick as honey, pouring through the arched windows where the lavender dusk usually sits. The whole station seems to glow from the inside out, as if the walls themselves are lit from within, and the floor—

The floor.

She looks down. The platform stretches ahead of her, freshly polished, and there in the surface — in the mirror-sheen of it, in the gleam that reflects the gold light back on itself like an infinity mirror — she sees them. Scuff marks. Dozens of them. Small, deliberate, repetitive. A pattern of wear that shouldn't exist because nobody comes here, nobody walks here, nobody has walked here in all the time she's been trapped between these walls.

Except someone has.

She follows the marks with her eyes. They form rectangles. Perfect rectangles, each one the same size, each one worn into the surface with the kind of repetition that speaks of hours. Many hours. Night after night after night, someone has stood on this platform and moved in these exact shapes. Forward. Side. Close. Back. Side. Close.

Box steps.

Evie's breath catches in her throat like a fishhook.

Box steps for a waltz.

And not just box steps. She crouches, runs her fingertips over the grooves, and feels the narrative they tell: the first rectangles are hesitant, uneven, the stride length inconsistent, the angles slightly off. Then they tighten. Correct. Perfect themselves. The later marks are precise, geometric, military in their regimentation. Whoever made these learned through sheer repetition, through stubbornness, through the kind of obsessive dedication that doesn't permit the luxury of failure.

She knows before she looks up. She knows with the same certainty she knows her own name, with the same bone-deep conviction she knows that the sun will rise (somewhere, even if not here), that these are his marks. These worn, private, desperately careful marks in the floor he must have polished himself — that's why it gleams, that's why the wax is fresh, because he wouldn't practice on a surface that wasn't perfect, because Julian Mercer does nothing by halves.

Julian.

He's standing at the far end of the platform, one hand resting on the record player's brass horn as if he's been caught mid-motion, mid-thought, mid-breath. His shirt sleeves are rolled to the elbow. His collar is undone. His hair — that perpetually flawless, categorically orderly hair — is mussed, as if he's been running his hands through it, which she has never seen him do, not once, not in all the weeks of his fastidious composure.

He looks, for the first time since she met him, like someone who wasn't assembled from a manual.

"It's fixed," she says, because her heart is doing something dangerous and she needs her mouth to do something ordinary.

He doesn't answer. His jaw is tight. His eyes are fixed somewhere over her left shoulder, which is where Julian Mercer's eyes go when he's calculating damage, when he's running risk assessments, when he's watching the world for threats that might disrupt the order of things.

She walks toward him, and each step brings more of the scuff marks into focus. They're everywhere. The entire platform is a palimpsest of secret practice, layered over and over until the floor has no choice but to remember. She counts — one, two, five, twelve separate rectangles, each wearing deeper into the polish, each more confident than the last.

No. Not more confident. More desperate.

These are the marks of a man who cannot fail at this, who has decided that this is something he will not fail at, who has poured the same exhausting perfectionism into learning to hold another person that he has poured into every KPI and quarterly review and soul-crushing spreadsheet of his former life. Not because he wants to be good at it. Because he's afraid of being bad at it. Because Julian Mercer's greatest fear has never been failure itself — it's being seen failing.

Evie stops three feet away from him. The waltz swells between them, an old French thing, a tune from a world that still had dances and seasons and people who touched each other without thinking about it.

"You've been practicing," she says. Not a question.

His jaw tightens incrementally. A micro-expression. A calibration.

"Dancing." The word lands flat and precise, the way all his words land, except this one has a hairline fracture in it. "You've been practicing *dancing*. Julian Mercer, spreadsheet king, has been doing *ballroom* in secret."

She says it with her whole chest. She says it with the full force of every ounce of disbelief and delight and sudden, overwhelming tenderness that is currently trying to crack her sternum open. She says it the way she says everything — too loud, too much, too honest — because she has never learned the art of keeping the feeling out of her voice and she doesn't want to learn now.

"By yourself. At night. On a platform at the end of the universe. Doing *box steps*."

The color rises. It starts at his collar and climbs — his neck, his jaw, the tips of his ears — until Julian Mercer, who has never flushed in his life, who has sat through hostile board meetings and performance reviews and one memorably catastrophic audit without a single betraying pixel of pink, turns the exact shade of the dusk that presses against the windows.

Lavender. He turns lavender.

"It's—" he starts, and stops. Swallows. Tries again. "The record player required recalibration. The mechanisms were—"

"Julian."

"The floor surface was suboptimal for—"

"*Julian.*"

He closes his mouth. She watches the war happen across his face — the need to explain, to justify, to categorize this into something sensible and contained and safe — and then she watches him lose. Something behind his eyes surrenders. Something lets go, some essential strut in the architecture of Julian Mercer's self-containment buckles under the weight of being known.

"Yes," he says quietly. "I've been practicing."

The waltz continues. The accordion breathes in and out, in and out, a mechanical lung, a clockwork heart. The gold light presses against the windows like it wants in, like it's been waiting outside for hours for this exact moment to arrive.

Evie opens her mouth to say something — something kind, probably, something that will make this easier, because that's what she does, she makes things easier for people even when they don't deserve it, even when they've spent weeks being infuriating and precise and emotionally walled off and unbearably, *unbearably* themselves—

But Julian moves first.

He crosses the distance between them in two strides and takes her hand.

Not gently. Not hesitantly. He takes it the way he takes everything — completely, decisively, as if he's reviewed the data and made an executive decision and filed the appropriate paperwork and now, *now* he is executing the plan.

"I believe," he says, and his voice is almost steady, almost the same distant monotone it always is, except for the tremor underneath it, the seismic waver of something enormous trying to hold itself together, "the protocol is to ask."

He doesn't ask.

He takes.

His other hand finds the small of her back — warm, impossibly warm, his palm pressing against the curve of her spine with the precise confidence of someone who has mapped this moment a hundred times in the dark — and he steps forward, and she steps back, and they are *dancing*.

Actually dancing.

Not the fumbling near-miss of before, not the clumsy reach-and-retreat of two people orbiting each other like satellites with no clearance. This is a waltz. A proper, measured, one-two-three waltz, and Julian Mercer leads it the way he does everything — precisely, precisely, with the grim focus of a man who has practiced until his feet bled and then practiced some more, because Julian Mercer does not do things by halves, Julian Mercer does not permit himself the luxury of incompetence, Julian Mercer apparently learned to waltz in secret at the end of the universe because—

Because of her.

The thought lands like a stone in still water and the ripples spread out and out and out, and she can't stop them, doesn't want to stop them, lets them wash through her until she's shaking with it, with the enormity of what he's done, of what he's been doing, alone, in the dark, while she slept.

She looks up at him. His face is close. The lavender hasn't faded from his cheeks but his eyes are steady now, fixed on hers with an intensity that makes her feel like she's being read, like she's a document he's memorizing, like she's the only data point that matters in a universe of infinite spreadsheets.

His hand is shaking.

She feels it against her back — the fine, barely-perceptible tremor running through his fingers, the vibration of a man who is holding himself together through sheer force of will and the knowledge that if he lets go he will shatter. He's terrified. He's absolutely terrified. And he's doing it anyway. And that — that is the most courageous thing she has ever seen another human being do.

He waltzes her down the platform. Past the record player, its brass horn catching the gold light and throwing it across the walls in spinning constellations. Past the benches, their wooden surfaces warm and glowing as if the station itself wants to help, wants to cradle this moment in its ancient, impossible hands. Past the stairs, where the Stationmaster sits on the top step, watching with huge amber eyes, its tail curled around its paws, purring so low and deep that Evie can feel it in the floor beneath her feet.

The waltz carries them. The accordion breathes. And the station holds them both inside it like cupping water in two hands, carefully, the way you hold something precious and impossible and unlikely to last.

And then the light changes.

Evie doesn't see it happen — she's looking at Julian, at the way his brow is furrowed in concentration, at the way his lips are slightly parted, at the way he's counting silently, *one-two-three, one-two-three*, the way he probably counts everything, the way he probably counted every single box step he practiced alone in the dark on a platform at the end of everything — but she feels it. The warmth on her skin shifts, intensifies, turns from gold to something richer, something that doesn't have a name because names are for things that exist and this light, this light is—

Extra.

The lavender dusk that presses against the arched windows turns. It turns gold. Not gradually, not the slow gradient of a setting sun, but deliberately, consciously, as if the station itself has made a decision. As if the impossible world outside has looked at the two of them — at this precise, terrified man and this loud, reckless woman waltzing across a platform at the end of everything — and decided that they deserve this. That for once, for this one moment, the universe will not be indifferent.

The shadows soften. The tiled walls seem to breathe. The vaulted ceiling arches above them like the inside of a heart and the whole impossible world holds its breath.

Julian spins her.

It's not in the practiced steps — she can tell, because his hold shifts, because there's a half-second of hesitation, because he's deviating from the plan and she can feel the exact moment he decides to trust himself enough to improvise — but he spins her anyway, one-two-three, and her feet leave the floor and her skirt flares and the gold light catches in her hair, and when she comes back to him she's laughing.

She's laughing and he's —

He's almost smiling.

Not the thin, polite compression of lips that passes for a smile on Julian Mercer's face. Not the performed courtesy, the contractual upturn, the grimace of social obligation. Something else. Something raw and reluctant and devastating. The corner of his mouth curving, the faintest softening around his eyes, the smallest possible crack in the wall he's built around himself, and it transforms him.

It transforms him *utterly*.

He looks like someone who could be happy.

He looks like someone who might have been happy once, a long time ago, before the spreadsheets and the KPIs and the endless performance reviews that measured everything that mattered and nothing that counted. He looks like someone who could stand in a station at the end of the universe and listen to a French waltz and hold a woman's hand and *smile*, actually smile, and mean it. A different man. The same man. The man he might have been all along, underneath all the protocols and procedures, if anyone had ever thought to ask.

Nobody asked. He didn't give them the chance.

But here, now, in a station that shouldn't exist, in a light that has no natural source, in a waltz played by a machine that was broken until a cat fixed it — here, he is giving her the chance. The most private thing he's ever done, and he is letting her see it.

He dips her.

It's the grand finale. The last move in the sequence, the one he must have practiced a hundred times with nothing but his own shadow for a partner, the one that requires trust — not in the steps, not in the count, but in the person you're holding. He dips her and her back arches over his arm and the gold light catches them both and she is still laughing, laughing up at the vaulted ceiling, laughing at the absurdity and the tenderness and the sheer improbable mathematics of this moment, and he's looking down at her with that almost-smile on his face and something in his eyes that she's never seen before, something that looks dangerously like wonder.

The Stationmaster purrs.

It's louder than it should be — a low, resonant vibration that seems to come from everywhere at once, from the walls and the floor and the air itself, as if the station is purring too, as if the whole impossible world is content, is satisfied, is finally, *finally* getting something right after so long getting everything wrong. The cat watches from the stairs, its amber eyes half-closed, its tail flicking in time with the waltz, and it is purring so loudly that Evie can feel it in her bones, in her blood, in the place where her heart is trying to beat in three-four time and mostly succeeding.

Julian pulls her up. She comes back to him in a cascade of breathless, ridiculous, incandescent joy, and she doesn't let go, and he doesn't let go, and the waltz keeps playing, and the gold light keeps glowing, and the station holds them in its impossible hands and does not let them fall.

For this one moment, at the end of everything, they are dancing.

It is enough.
