# Chapter 20

Cardboard. That was the thing—the sharp, dry fiber of corrugated cardboard breaking down at the seams, the glue gone brittle and powdery. Warehouse dust on top of that, the fine gray kind that settled into the creases of your hands and stayed. And under it all, like a note played too softly to name: the chemical tang of a photograph left too long in sunlight, its colors bleeding toward nothing.

Evie breathed it in before she understood what she was seeing.

The board glitched.

Not the stutter-flicker from before, not the momentary tear in the image. This was a full cascade, pixels rippling outward from the center like a stone dropped in water, and then the feed changed—replaced by something that made her stomach drop through the floor.

Her apartment.

Julian's apartment.

Side by side on the display, split-screened like before-and-after photos in a real estate listing. Reality's cameras had found their way inside, had panned across the rooms with the clinical steadiness of surveillance footage, and the rooms were—

Gone. Not destroyed. Not ransacked. Cleaned out. The way you clear a space when the person who lived there is never coming back.

"Evie," Julian said. Very quiet.

"I see it."

Her apartment first. The wide-plank floors she'd saved two years to afford, now bare of the rugs she'd collected from estate sales and flea markets. Walls stripped to primer white, the shadow outlines where her framed prints had hung like pale ghosts. The kitchen counter empty except for a ring stain where the espresso machine had lived. And the bookshelf—the one she'd built herself from reclaimed scaffolding boards and steel pipe brackets—completely bare, its shelves holding nothing but dust outlines of the books that had been there.

But the camera kept moving, and she saw the boxes.

They were stacked along the far wall of her living room, four columns of standard moving boxes, brown cardboard, packing tape going brown at the edges. And on each one, in black marker, block letters:

DONATE.

No hesitation mark. No question. Someone had gone through everything she owned and made the decision for her.

"Oh," she said. Just that. A small, involuntary sound.

The camera lingered on the boxes long enough for her to read the labels written in smaller script beneath the big directive. BOOKS. ART SUPPLIES — OILS/ACRYLICS. KITCHEN — MISC. CLOTHING — PROFESSIONAL. And on one box near the bottom of the stack, half-hidden by the box above it: FRAMED — DEGREE + CERTS.

Her degree. Her certifications. The things she'd hung in the hallway because she'd been proud of them, because earning them had cost her sleep and sanity and three years of dinners eaten standing over the kitchen sink. Someone had taken them off the wall, wrapped them in newspaper probably, and stuck them in a box marked for the nearest donation bin.

"Where's my—" She stopped. Started again. "My Le Creuset. The round one. French oven. It was my grandmother's."

The camera showed the kitchen box. She could see inside it—someone hadn't sealed the top flaps. Spatulas. A hand mixer. A baking sheet. No French oven. No round, flame-colored pot with the brass knob she'd polished every Sunday.

David. It had to be David. He'd always admired it. Made that joke—she remembered it now, the way it had seemed harmless at the time—that when they split up, he was taking the Le Creuset. She'd laughed. He hadn't.

She pressed her knuckles against her mouth and said nothing.

Then the feed shifted. Julian's apartment.

It was smaller than she'd expected. A studio, really, or a one-bedroom so narrow the bed could only fit against one wall. Neat. Ascetic. A single bookshelf, half the size of hers, and it had held books too—she could see the gaps—but also a chess set. The wooden kind with the built-in board, the pieces carved in some European style she didn't recognize.

But his boxes weren't labeled DONATE.

EVIDENCE.

That's what the black marker said. On every single one. EVIDENCE — BOOKS. EVIDENCE — PERSONAL EFFECTS. EVIDENCE — CORRESPONDENCE. EVIDENCE — PHOTOGRAPHS. The tape on his boxes was different too—not packing tape but the red-striped kind, the kind that meant tamper-evident seal, the kind federal agents use when they're bagging and tagging your entire life into an exhibit.

His books. His chess set. His photographs. Everything he'd ever touched or owned or arranged just so on his shelves was now a numbered item in a case file. His walls weren't just bare; they'd been photographed, measured, documented. The nail holes circled in marker. The outlet covers removed and bagged. His apartment wasn't emptied for donation, it was emptied for prosecution—and not his prosecution. He was the evidence. His life was the exhibit that proved someone else's guilt.

Julian sat very still beside her. She could feel the rigidity of him, the way he'd locked every muscle to keep from moving. His hands were flat on his thighs, fingers spread, pressing down so hard the tendons stood out.

"Julian," she said.

"I'm fine."

"You're not."

A long breath. He released it slowly, the way you do when you're trying not to scream. "No. I'm not."

The board flickered again, jumped, went back to the warehouse—the empty, timeless warehouse with its rows of shelves and its dust and its silence. The glitch was over. But the afterimage stayed, burned yellow-negative against her vision. DONATE. EVIDENCE. Two words for the same thing: the things you loved, sorted by strangers.

They didn't move apart. They'd been sitting close since the rain started—the cold, relentless rain that came through the warehouse's broken skylights as a fine mist, settling on everything, making the cardboard smell stronger and the dust stick to damp skin. She didn't remember deciding to sit this near him. It had just happened. Two people in a large, cold space, finding the one warm corner where the roof was intact and the concrete floor was dry, and not arguing about who needed more room.

Now the warmth between them was the only warm thing in the building. Radiating off his shoulder and her shoulder, almost touching, the air between them heated by two bodies that had no business being this close and no good reason to move apart.

Evie pulled her knees up and wrapped her arms around them. "My best friend helped her," she said.

Julian turned his head. She felt more than saw it—the shift of his attention, the way his breathing changed.

"Patricia," Evie went on. "You know that part. Patricia took the work. But she didn't do it alone. She had help. She had someone who knew which files were on my laptop, which drafts were in my cloud, which notes I kept in the pink notebook with the torn cover." She paused. "Someone who knew all of that because I told her. Because she was my best friend since sophomore year. Because we shared a dorm and then an apartment and then, when I thought I'd finally found my person in this world—the one you call at two a.m., the one who talks you off the ledge—her name was Grace."

She hadn't said the name out loud in months. It felt strange in her mouth. Like food you used to love that now makes you sick.

"Grace," Julian repeated. Quiet. Just holding the name.

"Grace Yang. We met in a lit class—Twentieth-Century American Novel. She was sitting next to me when the professor assigned *Beloved* and I started crying before he even finished the synopsis." A bare smile, here and gone. "She handed me a tissue and said, 'You're going to be okay. That book will wreck you, but you'll be okay.' And I believed her. I believed her for eight years."

The rain made a sound on the skylights like fingers drumming. Restless. Impatient.

"She was the only person I told when I first suspected something was wrong. When my notes showed up in Patricia's deck—not paraphrased, not inspired by, *copied*—the first person I called was Grace. And she said—" Evie's voice cracked, and she took a moment to collect it, to make it something she could control. "She said, 'That's crazy, Ev. Are you sure? Maybe you're being paranoid.' And I believed that too. Because she was Grace. Because Grace wouldn't lie to me."

Julian said nothing. That was the right thing. She knew it and he knew it. Any word would have been a wall, and she needed to walk through an open door.

"She was feeding Patricia everything. Every draft, every concept, every half-formed idea I trusted her with. And when it all came out—when I found the screenshots on Patricia's laptop that could only have come from inside my accounts—Grace didn't apologize. She didn't even look ashamed. She looked relieved." Evie's nails were digging into her own shins through her jeans. She forced herself to loosen them. "Like she'd been waiting to stop pretending."

"She screenshot you," Julian said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"Crying."

"Yes. I was—I'd gone to her apartment. After I found out. I was sitting on her kitchen floor, and I was crying, and she was holding me, and she had her phone in her other hand. Taking photos. And she posted them. She posted them on her private story with the caption: 'Can't handle the heat, stay out of the kitchen.' And then she tagged Patricia."

The warehouse was very quiet. Even the rain seemed to have softened, as if the building itself were leaning in to listen.

"She posted me crying on my best friend's kitchen floor and tagged the woman who'd stolen my work. And people liked it. People I knew. Colleagues. People from grad school." Evie rest her forehead on her knees. "That was the part that broke me. Not the theft. Not even the betrayal, exactly. It was the *audience*. The way my worst moment was content for them."

She heard Julian exhale. Slow and controlled. The kind of breath you take when you're sitting next to someone whose pain you can feel in your own chest, and you're trying not to make it about you.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Don't be sorry. It's not your fault."

"No. But I'm sorry it happened."

She lifted her head. The warehouse stretched out around them, empty and endless, and she thought: this is what it feels like inside a cleared-out apartment. This is the room they leave behind. Walls and floor and nothing else.

"Your turn," she said.

He didn't answer for long enough that she thought he wouldn't. That he'd do what he'd been doing since she met him—redirect, deflect, answer the question behind the question instead of the one she'd actually asked. She'd gotten good at reading the geometry of his evasions. The way he'd tilt his head when a question landed too close, the slight pause before he answered, the careful parsing of words that let him give you truth without giving you himself.

But this time he just sat there. Breathing. Staring at the board, which was showing nothing now—just the warehouse feed, the empty shelves, the dust settling.

"I've never told anyone this," he said.

"Okay."

"My mother didn't believe me."

Four words. Simple. Devastating. She heard them the way you hear a glass break in the next room—sharp, specific, already impossible to put back together.

"When I told her what Robert was doing. When I was fourteen and I finally—there was this moment where I thought, if I don't say it now, I'll never say it. So I said it. I said it in the kitchen, after school, while she was making dinner. I remember she was chopping onions. I remember the knife stopped and she just—" His voice went thin. "She just looked at me. And she said, 'Robert would never do that. You must have misunderstood.'"

Evie felt something inside her crack. A clean, deep fracture, the kind that goes all the way through.

"She said *misunderstood*," Julian repeated. "Like I'd misread a passage in a book. Like I'd gotten the wrong end of a conversation. Like what he'd been doing to me for two years was some kind of miscommunication."

He was still staring at the blank board. His jaw was set so tight she could see the muscle jumping.

"And I knew—I knew right then—that she knew. She knew and she was choosing not to know. Which is different, isn't it? Not believing because you genuinely can't accept it, that's one thing. But not believing because it's more convenient not to? Because believing would mean leaving, and leaving would mean starting over, and starting over would mean admitting you let it happen in the first place?" He finally moved—just his hands, lifting off his thighs and then settling back down, fingers curled now. "She chose him. She looked her fourteen-year-old son in the face and she chose the man who was hurting him, because the alternative was too expensive. Emotionally. Economically. Socially. Too expensive to believe your own child."

The rain had stopped. Evie noticed it like you notice a sound that's been there suddenly isn't—the absence more present than the thing itself. Water dripped from the skylights, regular as a metronome, counting out the seconds of silence.

"Julian."

"I'm fine."

"You keep saying that."

"Because I need to keep saying it. If I stop—" He broke off. Swallowed. "If I stop, I'll have to feel how not-fine I am. And I don't have time for that. There's a case. There's testimony. There's a chain of evidence and a hundred pages of deposition and if I fall apart now, after all of it—after I got this far—then what was it for? What was any of it for?"

She didn't reach for him. She understood, without being told, that touching him right now would be the wrong thing—not because he'd pull away but because he might not, and if he didn't, he'd hate himself for needing it.

"You asked me what I'm afraid of," she said instead. "That night at the bar, before we came here. You asked."

"I remember."

"I'm afraid that Grace was right. That I can't handle the heat. That the reason I'm in this warehouse with you instead of in a courtroom or a boardroom or anywhere that matters is because I was never strong enough to be in those rooms in the first place."

Julian turned to look at her. Really look, for the first time since the board had glitched. His eyes were dark and steady and she couldn't read them, which was worse than if she could have.

"I'm afraid," he said, "that my mother was right too. That she saw something true when she looked at me and decided I wasn't worth believing. That there's something in me that makes people choose other people instead. That Robert saw it and she saw it and maybe everyone sees it, and the reason I'm alone is—" He stopped himself. Ground his teeth. "The reason I'm alone is because I'm the kind of person people leave."

They sat with it. The two fears, side by side on the cold floor of a warehouse that existed outside of time, in the silence after rain. Her fear of being too weak to matter. His fear of being too broken to keep. Both of them betrayed not by enemies or strangers but by the people nearest to them—the people who were supposed to hold the door, not push them through it.

"Maybe that's why we're here," Julian said. His voice had lost its courtroom edges. He sounded like what he was: a man sitting on a concrete floor, out of moves. "There's nothing left to go back to."

Evie looked at the board. The empty shelves. The dust. The warehouse that kept showing them nothing, because nothing was what was left.

"Maybe there never was," she said.

They didn't touch. They didn't need to. The space between them had narrowed to inches—the width of a breath, the depth of a pause—and the warmth their bodies made filled that space until it wasn't empty anymore. It wasn't comfort, exactly. It wasn't solace. It was two people sitting close enough in a cold room that the air between them became its own kind of contact. Its own kind of proof.

See? We're still here. We're still warm.

That's something. That has to be something.

Outside, the rain started again.
