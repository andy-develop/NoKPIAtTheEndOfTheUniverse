# Chapter 21: GODOT — ETA: 7 DAYS

The cold came first.

It always did on the platform — that particular cold that wasn't weather so much as absence, the temperature of a place that had given up on being heated. It pressed against her skin through the wool of her shawl, finding the gaps between threads with the precision of something that had been practicing for a very long time. Beneath the wool and beneath the cold, she could smell the metallic tang of the platform itself — ozone and iron and something sweeter underneath, like copper pennies left in the rain.

And underneath all of that, closer than it should have been, the warmth of another body.

Julian sat beside her on the bench, close enough that she could feel the radiant heat of his arm without touching it. He'd been sitting closer lately. Neither of them had acknowledged it. It was one of those things that existed in the space between words, like the station itself — present, undeniable, and thoroughly unmentioned.

The announcement board flickered.

Evie had stopped looking at the board weeks ago. It cycled through the same useless information: train delays, platform changes in languages she couldn't read, occasionally a poem fragment that might have been accidental or might have been the station's idea of a joke. The board was part of the scenery now, like the clock that had been lying about the time since she arrived — 6:00 AM, it said, as it always said, as it would probably say forever.

But the flicker was different this time.

She felt it before she saw it — a change in the hum. The platform had a constant low vibration, a frequency that lived in the benches and the tiles and the teeth. It shifted, just slightly, just enough that her body registered the change before her mind caught up. A new note in the chord. A sound like something powering up, or powering down, or simply deciding.

She looked up.

The board was clear. Not cycling. Not glitching. Not showing a poem fragment or a delay notice or a menu from a canteen that didn't exist. It showed one thing, sharp and steady against the dark screen:

**GODOT — ETA: 7 DAYS**

Evie stopped breathing.

Beside her, Julian went rigid. She felt it — the sudden tension in his body, the way his warmth turned to something harder, like metal cooling after being forged. His coffee cup hung in the air between sips, forgotten.

The station had never given them a timeline before. The station had never given them anything concrete, anything measurable, anything with a number in it. The clock lied. The board joked. The cat spoke in riddles and the tea materialized without explanation and the fog never cleared and nothing, nothing, nothing had ever come with a date attached.

Seven days.

The cat, who had been sleeping in a perfect croissant shape on the opposite bench, uncurled itself with the slow precision of a creature that had never once been surprised in its entire life but was now choosing to appear alert. It sat up. Its tail wrapped around its paws. Its golden eyes moved from the board to Evie to Julian and back to the board, as if re-reading the message to make sure it hadn't misheard.

"Seven days," Julian said. Not a question. A confirmation. His voice had changed already — tighter, sharper, the voice of a man who had just been handed a deadline and was already assembling a spreadsheet in his mind.

Evie said nothing. She was watching the board the way you watch a wound, waiting to see if it would close or keep bleeding.

The letters didn't change. The number didn't flicker. GODOT — ETA: 7 DAYS. As solid as anything had ever been on this platform.

Julian set his coffee down. The ceramic clicked against the bench and the sound was too loud, too specific, too much like a sound that belonged to a world where things happened and time moved and seven days meant something.

"Right," he said, and he stood up.

Evie watched him walk toward the wardrobe. The strange, impossible wardrobe that had been producing clothes and toiletries and, once, a very specific brand of lemon biscuits she hadn't been able to find in years. The door opened for him before he touched it, which was either polite or ominous.

He came back with his suit.

It was clean. Not just clean — dry-cleaned, pressed, the lapels sharp enough to cut paper. The wardrobe had done this overnight, quietly, while they slept, as if it had always known this day was coming. As if it had been preparing.

Julian laid the suit on the bench between them and began to undress without ceremony. He was already somewhere else — Evie could see it in the way he moved, efficient and purposeful, none of the easy looseness he'd developed over the past weeks. The man who had taught her to make tea by feel, who had laughed at the cat's riddles, who had sat close enough to share warmth without mentioning it — that man was folding himself away like the casual clothes he was taking off.

Back came the analyst. The problem-solver. The man with a plan.

His fingers did up the buttons of his shirt with practiced speed. He adjusted his cuffs. He straightened his tie, and the tie was the same one he'd arrived in, but it looked different now — less like fabric and more like armor.

"Julian," Evie said.

He didn't look up. He was checking his pockets — the ones in his suit jacket, the ones in his trousers, inventorying what he had and what he needed.

"Julian."

"I need to make a list," he said. His voice was steady. That was the worst part. Steady like the board. Steady like the number seven. "We need to figure out what we're taking, what we're leaving, whether the train will have —"

"A list," she repeated.

"Yes. A list. We have seven days, Evie. That's a timeline. That's the first concrete piece of information this place has ever given us, and I'm not going to waste it sitting on a bench waiting to see if it changes its mind."

He said *us* but he meant *himself*. She could hear it. The *us* was a kindness, a formality, something he was saying because it was what you said. The truth was in his hands — already moving, already sorting, already reaching for the future while his body was still standing on the platform of the present.

Evie pulled her shawl tighter.

The wool caught the cold and held it close to her skin, and she let it. She wanted the cold. She wanted to feel exactly where she was, which was on a bench, on a platform, in a station that had just announced a departure date like it was a flight board at an airport, as if any of this was normal, as if seven days wasn't both an eternity and no time at all.

She didn't pack.

She watched him pack. She watched him move through the space they'd shared — the bench where they'd sat, the corner where the cat slept, the spot near the vending machine where he'd once spent forty-five minutes trying to get it to dispense something other than black coffee, ultimately producing a packet of raisins that they'd split while the cat looked on with undisguised disdain. She watched him move through all of it like it was already behind him.

Her heart did something she didn't have a word for. Cracked open, maybe. Like a macaron shell — fragile and sweet and designed to shatter at the slightest pressure. The inside was soft. That was the part she didn't want him to see.

So she sat. She wrapped the shawl around her shoulders and pulled the ends across her chest and she sat on that bench like it was the last solid thing in the world, and she watched Julian Mercer prepare to leave.

The cat landed on the bench beside her without a sound.

It didn't do the thing cats do where they announce themselves with a meow or a head-butt. It simply appeared, the way it appeared everywhere — suddenly, impossibly, as if it had always been there and she'd only just noticed. Its tail swished once, twice, then settled.

"The train comes when it comes," the cat said. Its voice was the same as always — deep, unhurried, faintly bored. "The countdown is decorative. Or isn't. Who can say?"

Evie looked at it. The cat looked at the board, where GODOT — ETA: 7 DAYS still glowed like a verdict.

"Seven days," she said.

"The board has said many things. Most of them were untrue. Some of them were poetry. Once it displayed the recipe for a very competent choucroute garnie." The cat's ear flicked. "The point is that the station has a sense of narrative. Whether it has a sense of timing is another question entirely."

"So it might not be seven days."

"It might be seven days. It might be seven minutes. It might be that Godot has always been here, waiting behind the fog, and the board is simply catching up." The cat yawned, showing every single one of its teeth. "The station keeps its own schedule. It always has."

Evie pulled the shawl tighter. The wool was warm now, warmed through by her body, but she still felt the cold underneath. The cold that was the platform, the station, the space between her and the tracks.

"You've already decided, though," the cat said.

It wasn't a question. The cat's golden eyes found hers, and there was something in them that wasn't amusement for once. Something that might have been gentleness, or might have been the look you give someone when you already know the ending of their story.

"Haven't you?" the cat said.

Evie didn't answer.

The cat didn't need her to.

It stood, stretched elaborately — spine curving, back arching, every vertebra announcing itself — and padded away toward the far end of the platform, where the fog was thickest and the tracks disappeared into nothing. It didn't look back. It didn't need to do that either.

Evie sat on the bench and pulled her knees up and wrapped her shawl around everything and watched Julian make his lists and pack his bags and become the man he'd been before this place got hold of him, and she did not decide, because she had already decided, and decisions you've already made don't need to be spoken aloud to be real.

---

That night, she couldn't sleep.

The bench was the same bench it had always been, which was to say it was slightly too short and slightly too hard and exactly the right temperature for existential discomfort. She lay on it with her eyes open and watched the ceiling, which was high and vaulted and covered in tiles that might have been white once but had settled into a comfortable grey. The clock said 6:00 AM. The clock always said 6:00 AM. The clock was a liar and had always been a liar and tonight, of all nights, she found that comforting.

She got up.

The platform was different at night — not darker, exactly, because the station had no respect for the difference between day and night, but quieter. The hum was lower. The fog was thicker. The announcement board still glowed: GODOT — ETA: 6 DAYS now, because of course it had ticked over at midnight like a proper countdown, because of course it was keeping perfect time even though the clock above it couldn't be bothered.

She walked to the platform edge.

The tracks disappeared into fog in both directions, which was how they'd always been. She'd stopped wondering what was beyond the fog weeks ago. The fog was the fog. It didn't answer questions; it swallowed them. She stood at the yellow line — painted, chipped, presumably indicating where it was safe to wait for a train that never came — and she looked into the nothing, and the nothing looked back.

"You're up."

Julian's voice. Behind her.

She turned. He was standing a few feet away, and he was holding her shawl. Her shawl, which she had left folded on the bench, which he must have picked up on his way over. He held it out to her, and the gesture was so simple and so certain that it broke something in her chest — not painfully, just precisely, like a key turning in a lock she hadn't known was there.

He must have known she'd come. He must have known she wouldn't sleep. He must have been lying on his own bench, staring at his own ceiling, watching his own clock lie to him, and at some point he must have thought: *She'll go to the edge.* And then: *She'll be cold.* And then: *I'll bring the shawl.*

The chain of reasoning was so him. So analyst. So problem-solver. So much the man who made lists and packed bags and straightened ties like armor. And underneath all of that, underneath the logic, was something else — the knowledge of her that had nothing to do with logic and everything to do with the weeks they'd spent sharing this platform, this cold, this impossible waiting room at the end of everything.

She took the shawl.

Their fingers touched, briefly, and his were warm and hers were not, and the contact lasted exactly as long as it needed to and no longer.

She wrapped the shawl around her shoulders. He stepped up beside her at the platform edge, and now they were both standing at the yellow line, looking into the fog.

The fog didn't clear. It never did. But standing there, with the cold in her lungs and the wool around her shoulders and his body warm and close beside her — closer than the bench, closer than before, close enough that she could feel the rhythm of his breathing — the fog seemed less like a wall and more like a curtain. Something that could be pulled back. Something that might, on the other side, reveal a train.

Or might not.

Her hand moved without her permission. It found his, in the space between their bodies, and she felt his fingers close around hers — not tightly, not desperately, just certainly. The way you hold a hand that you know. The way you hold on when the only thing you're certain of is the person beside you.

They stood at the edge of the tracks and looked into the fog where a train may or may not have been coming in six days or seven or never, and neither of them spoke, because there was nothing to say that the silence wasn't already saying better.

The clock overhead read 6:00 AM.

Somewhere behind them, behind the benches and the wardrobes and the vending machines with their terrible coffee, the cat was watching from the shadows with its golden eyes, and if it was smiling, well — cats always look like they're smiling. It's one of their gifts.

The board glowed.

GODOT — ETA: 6 DAYS.

And Evie Chen held Julian Mercer's hand, and the fog did not part, and the train did not come.

Not yet.
